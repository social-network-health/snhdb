#### **Honoring Elizabeth Carnegie: A Pathfinder in Shaping the Discipline of Nursing**

A few weeks ago, I had a discussion with junior level faculty who were on practice and teaching appointment tracks at their respective universities. They wondered if they should be engaged in nursing scholarship because they were hired primarily to teach. Immediately, I was reminded of my first encounter with Dr. Mary Elizabeth Carnegie. Our Dean, Dr. Gloria Smith, hired Dr. Carnegie to mentor junior faculty in writing for publication. At the time, I was an assistant professor at the University of Oklahoma. One of my peers asked, "Why does publishing really matter to us we just teach?" We were not informed about who Dr. Carnegie was and her contributions to nursing. Dr. Carnegie looked at us with a smile and a twinkle in her eyes. She responded, "When we publish innovative ideas, evidence informed approaches to practice and teaching and original groundbreaking research, we advance the discipline of nursing and contribute to the body of knowledge. This distinguishes nursing from an occupation to a discipline. When you publish, your classroom becomes the world. This expands your reach of influence beyond the walls of single classroom."

In my conversations with her, I learned that Dr. Carnegie established the Bachelor of Science in Nursing Program at Hampton University in 1943; held an appointment as Dean of the Florida A & M University School of Nursing between 1945 to 1953; and, she served as the first editor of the highly

 acclaimed Nursing Research and editor of Nursing Outlook, Dr. Carnegie created a path for all of us to travel.

Dr. Carnegie created a path for all of us to travel. Dr. Carnegie was actively engaged in the Association of Black Nursing Faculty (ABNF) serving as a mentor to many junior faculty members. Her book, The Path We Tread, documents the achievements of Black American Nurses. At an ABNF conference in Kansas City, she provided members with autographed copies of her book. I cherish my book copy and I am humbled that I got to know Dr. Carnegie on a first-hand basis. In this issue of the ABNFF Journal, writers present on the Experiences and challenges for African American women with self-reported hypertension during the COVID-19 pandemic, Impact of COVID-19 on Recruitment in a Community-Based Breast Cancer Awareness Project, Voices of Resilience: An Autoethnographic Exploration of the Contributions of Black Nursing Faculty in Nursing Education and Expanding Educational Opportunities for Nurses: Faculty and Student Reflections of a HyFlex Pilot Program. The first two articles provide implications for nursing practice and the third and fourth articles have implications for nursing education.

The ABNFF Journal continues the path set by Dr. Elizabeth Carnegie and makes a significant contribution to the discipline of nursing.

Spotlight on Healthcare, Nursing Education and Population Health Issues

This first issue of the 2024 ABNFF Journal highlights research and manuscripts on healthcare, nursing education, and population health issues that impact African-American communities. All articles collectively contribute to the understanding and improving healthcare practices, education, and outcomes for marginalized populations.

Findings of Dr. Alexander and her team suggest that nursing students value scheduling flexibility and that HyFlex courses require technological support and efficient communication between instructors and students. The research supports the continued and expanded use of HyFlex modalities to promote adult student retention in nursing education.

Dr. Iheduru-Anderson and colleagues captured the lived experience of Black academic nurses through an autoethnographic qualitative study. Researchers found four (4) major themes: mentoring, a culture of oppression, a sense of duty, and breaking the cycle to build a legacy, along with thirteen subthemes. Understanding these experiences can guide the optimization of work culture and contribute to diversity, equity, and inclusivity science.

Grove et al. (2024) studied African-American women managing hypertension during the

COVID-19 pandemic. They found that African-American women living with hypertension faced increased obstacles in managing their condition during the COVID-19 pandemic, leading to higher risks for adverse events related to hypertension.

Dr. Jerome-D'Emilia and colleagues reported on their breast cancer awareness project, "Sister Give Me Your Hand." This program recruited church lay navigators to increase breast cancer awareness in Black communities. They reported that Black women have lower rates of breast cancer diagnosis compared to White women but are more likely to die from the disease. Early detection through screening mammography is essential, but disparities exist in screening. Building relationships with trusted community members, such as leaders in faith-based organizations, is critical to engaging women in educational programs about breast cancer. The COVID-19 pandemic highlighted the need to improve this population's internet access and digital health literacy.

McNeill presented an evidence-based practice (EBP) course containing Master of Nursing (MSN), Doctor of Nursing Practice (DNP) and Doctor of Philosophy (PhD) nursing students (N=76) offered during a 12-week Spring/Summer semester. Her team assessed the effectiveness and feasibility of a combined graduate level EBP course. The results indicated a significant difference pre vs post course in the areas of EBP use, EBP skill and EBP communication. However, 22 out of the 24 students that reported feeling overwhelmed by the course were MSN students (91.7%).

And the pilot study by Okyere and her team explored the use and feasibility of the Newest Vital Sign Health Literacy assessment tool in a primary care clinic. Results suggest that over half of patients had adequate health literacy, while a significant portion showed limited health literacy. Clinic nursing staff found the tool reliable and valuable for modifying treatment plans.

This issue contains the work of authors with topics of varied importance. As you read their work, consider how change might look in the classroom, in clinics, and communities. Additionally, consider next steps in building knowledge around a topic of your choice. The possibilities are enormous.

Applying Freire's Critical Pedagogy to Nursing Education

Paulo Freire's Pedagogy of the Oppressed is a transformative text that critiques traditional education systems and proposes a new, liberatory approach to learning. Freire's ideas challenge the "banking model" of education, where students are passive recipients of knowledge, and instead promote a dialogic exchange where learners and educators engage in critical reflection and action (Freire, 2000). Freire's Pedagogy of the Oppressed has profoundly impacted education, inspiring a shift towards critical pedagogy that empowers students and challenges oppressive structures. While Freire's ideas have been widely applied in K-12 and higher education, nursing education has been slower in embracing this transformative approach. It is time for nursing programs to critically examine their curricula and teaching methods through the lens of Freire's critical pedagogy.

Freire's central argument is that traditional "banking" models of education, where teachers deposit information into passive students, perpetuate oppression by treating students as objects rather than subjects of their own learning process, discouraging critical thinking and maintaining the status quo

(Freire, 2000, 2018). In contrast, critical pedagogy encourages students to question, challenge, and transform their realities. Critical pedagogy encourages students to critically examine power dynamics, inequities, and the social determinants of health. This fosters a sense of agency and commitment to addressing systemic issues in healthcare. As nursing educators, it is our responsibility to move beyond simply transmitting facts and skills to cultivating nurses who are agents of change, capable of identifying and addressing systemic inequities in healthcare. By creating a dialogical learning environment, we can empower students to be active participants in their learning and future practice. This sense of agency can inspire nurses to become advocates for their patients and work towards social justice in healthcare. We can promote dialogue and reflection by creating spaces for open dialogue and reflection, where students can share their experiences, challenge assumptions, and learn from each other (Rivers, 2020). This can foster a deeper understanding of the complex social and cultural factors that influence health outcomes.

One fundamental tenet of Freire's critical pedagogy is problem-posing education, where students and teachers engage in dialogue to uncover and analyze real-world problems (Freire, 2018). This cultivates the ability to think critically, ask questions, and develop innovative solutions (Fields et al., 2022). In nursing education, this could involve case studies that explore the social determinants of health, the impact of racism and discrimination on patient outcomes, and the role of nurses in advocating for marginalized communities (Matthews, 2014; Maviglia, 2019). By grappling with these complex issues, nursing students will better understand the social, political, and economic factors that shape healthcare and their practice. Nurse educators can empower students as co-creators of knowledge through participatory learning activities, such as community-based projects, where students work collaboratively to address real-world health challenges.

Another crucial aspect of Freire's pedagogy is conscientization, the process of developing a critical consciousness of one's social reality through reflection and action (praxis) and recognizing one's

power to transform oppressive structures (Freire, 2018). Nursing education should create opportunities for students to reflect on their biases, privileges, and experiences and how these shape their perceptions of patients and healthcare systems (Rivers, 2020; Treinen et al., 2022). By fostering self-awareness and a commitment to social justice, nursing programs can prepare graduates to challenge the status quo and work towards more equitable and inclusive healthcare. Freire's concept of praxis can be applied to nursing education by promoting reflective practice, where students consider the implications of their actions and the context of their work. This can be achieved through reflective journals, where students document and analyze their clinical experiences. This self-awareness is crucial for providing culturally sensitive, patient-centered care and advocating for marginalized communities.

Freire's pedagogy aims to achieve liberation, not just for the oppressed but also for the oppressors, through an inclusive, dialogic pedagogy that promotes critical thinking (Fields et al., 2022; Freire, 2000, 2018). Freire emphasizes the importance of praxis, the integration of reflection and action. In nursing education, this means providing students with opportunities to engage in community-based projects, advocacy initiatives, and other forms of experiential learning that put their critical thinking and problem-solving skills into practice (Fields et al., 2022). By bridging the gap between theory and practice, nursing programs can help students develop a sense of agency and the confidence to be change agents in their future careers. To address the social determinants of health, students must understand and address the social, economic, and political contexts that shape people's lives. By incorporating this perspective, nursing education can prepare students to recognize and respond to the social determinants of health. This will involve integrating health equity, social justice, and advocacy content into the curriculum.

Freire's Pedagogy of the Oppressed offers a transformative vision for education, one that empowers students to become critical thinkers and agents of change. This transformative learning is not just about acquiring new knowledge, but also about experiencing a shift in worldview and sense of self. By incorporating Freire's ideas into nursing education, we can create a learning environment that not only imparts clinical skills but also fosters a deep sense of social responsibility and commitment to equitable healthcare. This is not just a theoretical concept, but a practical approach that can have a profound impact on nursing practice. By fostering critical thinking, promoting dialogue, and encouraging action, nursing programs can empower students to challenge oppressive structures to address health disparities. As we strive to prepare the next generation of nurses, Freire's pedagogy serves as a guiding light, reminding us of the transformative power of education in creating a more just and equitable world. It is time for nursing education to take a bold step forward and apply Freire's transformative ideas to nursing.

## Expanding Educational Opportunities for Nurses: Faculty and Student Reflections of a HyFlex Pilot Program

#### Introduction

 Despite the increasing number of registered nurses (RNs) in the United States over the past decade, the nursing shortage still requires attention (NCSBN, 2023; US Bureau of Labor Statistics, 2022). Multiple regions of the United States (U.S.) anticipate shortfalls in their regional nursing workforce (Auerbach et al., 2017). COVID-19 appears to have accelerated this trend, exacerbating many pre-existing workforce issues (Haas et al., 2020). For example, Martin and colleagues (2023) examined data from over 50,000 nurses (including registered nurses, advanced practice registered nurses, and licensed practical nurses) across 45 states and found that 62% reported increased workload during the COVID-19 pandemic. In addition to increased workloads, the nurses in this study reported higher levels of burnout and stress and increased incidences of feeling emotionally drained and fatigued. Such issues were particularly evident among RNs with ten years or less experience (Martin et al., 2023).

Prior to the COVID-19 pandemic, nurse educators, researchers, and professionals specifically called attention to the shortage of baccalaureateprepared nurses. For example, the Institute of Medicine's (2011) 's landmark report on "The Future of Nursing" declared the need for more baccalaureate-prepared nurses in the workforce, recommending that the fieldwork reach at least 80% by 2020. Unfortunately, the nursing workforce has not met this goal. According to the recent workforce survey by the National Council of State Boards of Nursing, 65.2% of registered nurses are prepared at the baccalaureate or graduate level. Attention to this issue is important because RN to Bachelor of Science Nursing (BSN) programs have been highlighted as pathways for career advancement (AACN, 2021; NAM, 2021), and research shows that BSN degree

holders strengthen healthcare settings' ability to care for patients (AACN, 2021; NAM, 2021).

As evidenced by declines in BSN program enrollment, challenges remain in meeting the educational needs of RNs seeking to complete their undergraduate degrees. According to the American Association of Colleges of Nursing (2022), "From 2020 to 2021, enrollments decreased by 9.6 percent, and graduations were down by 1.5 percent. This recent downward trend follows an enrollment surge in RN to BSN programs, rapidly increasing from 30,684 students in 2002 to 139,587 enrolled students in 2018 (a 355% increase)" (p.2). Innovations in nursing education, specifically instructional options and course delivery, are needed to expand the options for registered nurses (RNs) seeking to complete Bachelor of Science in Nursing (BSN) degrees.

Virtual learning represents one approach to innovating nursing instruction, as online course modalities have been shown to improve access and enrollment (Beatty, 2019). Despite the growing popularity of online programs, nurse educators must consider the multiplicity of learning styles, including those who benefit from or prefer in-person learning interactions. Though hybrid courses provide a mix of in-person and online participation, scheduling in this modality can still pose challenges, especially for working students (Beatty, 2019). Hybrid Flexible or HyFlex approaches are designed to address these challenges by giving students both the option to learn in-person and online and the choice to alternate modalities as needed.

#### Significance of the Study

The HyFlex learning option allows added flexibility for students to register for required nursing courses and choose whether to attend face-to-face or online, synchronously or asynchronously, all within the same academic course. As virtual and hybrid course options become more commonplace in

nursing education, more research is needed to support the continued development of these instructional modalities.

The University of Houston-Clear Lake IRB reviewed the human subject research protocol and consent form(s). This study was determined to be Exempt Research (Category 4). This study aimed to examine faculty experiences developing HyFlex courses and students' perspectives and experiences as participants in the RN-BSN program's HyFlex pilot at the University of Houston-Clear Lake. Multiple research questions guided the study, including: 1) What was the process for UHCL faculty to develop HyFlex courses? 2) What motivated nursing students to enroll in the HyFlex pilot? 3) What are pilot participants' perceptions of and experiences with HyFlex courses? And 3) How do pilot participants think HyFlex courses could be improved?

#### Principles of Hybrid Flexible (HyFlex) Course Design

To appreciate the HyFlex option and the study results, one must understand the four principles of HyFlex (Beatty, 2023; Kelly, 2020). Beatty (2019) established four fundamental principles to guide the use of the modalities. Each principle is summarized below:

Principle 1 – Learner Choice: HyFlex provides meaningful alternative participation modes and enables students to choose between participation modes weekly or by topic, providing the highest flexibility within a course (Whalley et al., 2021). A student who prefers to learn entirely online may never attend a class session or choose to come to class in person or through Zoom. The key to this principle is that the student can decide how to access the course on a session-by-session, week-by-week basis.

Principle 2 – Equivalency: HyFlex provides equivalent learning activities in all participation modes. For a HyFlex course to be equivalent, all content and activities in all modalities must lead to equivalent assessments and learning outcomes (Whalley et al., 2021). The instructor is responsible for ensuring that students in all three modalities are taught the concepts in a way that produces similar outcomes. Equivalency does not, however, imply equality as an online learning experience (i.e.,

asynchronous discussion) may be less socially interactive than a classroom-based discussion activity. In each case, students should be challenged to reflect upon learning content, contribute their developing ideas to the discussion, and interact with the ideas of their peers. Providing equivalent learning experiences in various modes may be one of the most significant challenges in the HyFlex approach (Whalley et al., 2021).

Principle 3 – Reusability: This principle demonstrates how HyFlex should ensure all students have equal access to the course and course materials across modalities and reinforce learning (Whalley et al., 2021). Instructors should provide learning activities used across modalities to all students. For example, if a discussion board is available for asynchronous learners, face-to-face learners should also have access to it. Conversely, if students watch a video in class, the video should be posted into the Course Management Platform (CMP) to be accessed by all learners. For instance, a student who has attended class face-to-face should be able to go home and re-watch the video of the lecture and review concepts learned in class.

Principle 4 – Accessibility: This is particularly important in a HyFlex course to ensure that students are provided with technology skills and that full access to instructional resources and activities in all participation modes is essential (Whalley et al., 2021). For example, the instructor would ensure the course links are correctly identified, and images have alternative text. Furthermore, the instructor should ensure students can access the material and have the required technology to ensure alternative participation modes.

#### Hybrid vs. HyFlex Models

Hybrid learning integrates complementary face-to-face and online learning experiences to serve intended learning objectives. The HyFlex model differs from the hybrid. Hybrid students are expected to undergo the same combination of online and inperson activities. In contrast, HyFlex combines "hybrid" and "flexible." Brian Beatty introduced HyFlex learning in 2006 at San Francisco State University (Beatty, 2019). An actual hyperflex class will offer three different modalities simultaneously, allowing the learner to choose between those

modalities on a session-by-session basis (Whalley et al., 2021). Beatty suggests asynchronous or fully online, synchronous engagement via a mobile streaming platform like Zoom and face-to-face instruction (Lohmann et al., 2021).

#### Reflections on Developing the HyFlex Nursing

#### Pilot at UH-Clear Lake

To answer the study's first research question, the authors, who are nursing faculty in the University of Houston-Clear Lake (UHCL) Bachelor of Science in Nursing (BSN) program, recounted their experiences designing and executing the HyFlex pilot. The following section provides insight into the background and motivations for incorporating HyFlex options into the RN-BSN curriculum and their approach to developing the HyFlex pilot nursing courses.

#### Background and Motivation

Established in 2014, the BSN program at UHCL started as a traditional program offering inperson courses. Historically, most UHCL BSN students work regular hours as RNs in their respective communities across Houston and outlying areas. Though the program has long considered offering alternate course modalities to meet the needs of working students, the COVID-19 Pandemic required the program to go bimodal, offering courses online and—where possible—face-to-face. The pandemic caused increasing numbers of students to seek employment as traveling nurses, and student demands for varied learning options became even greater.

To meet the needs of students and the rapidly changing nursing workforce, the UHCL nursing program faculty, with the support of the university, developed a pilot of the HyFlex program during the Fall 2020 semester. Student feedback and interest in participating in the HyFlex courses were positive, and more than 30 students opted for the pilot.

#### Developing the HyFlex Pilot Courses

HyFlex course(s) design allows students to choose how they complete course activities in any given week (or, in some cases, per topic). RN-BSN nursing faculty collaborated with the Office of Instructional Design and Technology in the university's Office of Information Technology (OIT). The nursing faculty designed the basic shell of each course with the guidance of OIT. Each course is then updated to include information about the instructor, course, resources available to support the course, and a course orientation. See Appendix A for the complete list of courses developed.

To create courses with all three modalities rolled into one, instructors approached the course development in three ways: prepping as though it were a completely asynchronous online course, an in-person course, and a synchronous online course (see Table 1). This process ensured that students had full access to all course materials regardless of their chosen modality. All pilot courses were also required to pass the department's Quality Assurance (QA) standards.

In HyFlex courses, various learning activities typically occurring in classrooms are presented in a virtual format for online students. Activities included podcasts, video recordings, discussion files, and handouts. These activities are available for online and in-person students who desire access after completing the class session. The activities completed by online students, such as chats, asynchronous discussion posts, and peer feedback, became meaningful learning resources for in-person students and provided helpful review materials for online students. (see table 1).

#### **Additional Considerations**

Like most university initiatives, expanding course options requires resources and investments in capacity building. Additional funding may be required to provide administrators and faculty with the resources needed to implement the modality. Examples of costs associated with implementation include computers equipped with camera access, speakers, and software to ensure lectures are captured.

In addition to hardware and software equipment, students and faculty need various supportive services to succeed across HyFlex modalities. Such services include technology support services such as the IT helpdesk. The pilot program students relied on support from the university library, which provided online chat and 24-hour messaging services, and the campus writing center, which provided walk-in hours and scheduled appointments

for students online and in-person. In HyFlex courses, online learners and faculty must possess specific technological skills. Professional development is also needed for faculty to learn the theoretical foundation of hybrid online teaching, practical teaching methods and techniques, and how to solve technological issues during course sessions.

Another important reflection on implementing HyFlex is that students must have reliable internet access to the virtual course option. For instance, if a student is not physically capable of attending class, then in-class participation is not an option for that student. If a student does not have convenient and reliable internet access, online participation may not be a realistic option.

#### Students' Reflections and Experiences with HyFlex Pilot Courses

To answer the second and third research questions, the authors created a student questionnaire to collect data on HyFlex pilot participants' motivations for enrolling, views on the advantages and challenges of HyFlex, and how they perceive the model to be useful in advancing nursing education and practice. The questionnaire consisted of four open-ended questions: 1) Why did you choose the HyFlex course modality? 2), What are the advantages and challenges of HyFlex learning? 3) What suggestions do you have for improving HyFlex courses? and 3) How can the nursing workforce meet the needs of healthcare settings?

Thirty-one students in the BSN-RN program at UHCL participated in the yearlong pilot (fall 2021 spring 2022). The questionnaire was sent to 21 enrolled students via email in 2023. Twelve students, both men and women, aged 23-50 across multiple races, submitted responses. Relying largely on aspects of naturalistic inquiry (Guba & Lincoln, 1985), faculty examined the data, recorded initial impressions and hunches associated with student responses, and interpreted the meaning students gave to each question.

#### Findings

The analysis of questionnaire data revealed that, overall, HyFlex courses yield a positive experience for nursing students across the four HyFlex principles of learner choice, equivalence, reusability, and accessibility. Comments related to time, communication, technological resources, and organization were identified as significant patterns in students' responses. More specifically, time or the notion that nursing students value the flexibility in scheduling and course options that enhance their ability to better balance school, work, and home were identified as strengths. Conversely, technology specifically access to technology resources, was cited as a challenge or barrier. Furthermore, regarding communication, students conveyed that HyFlex courses require efficient mechanisms for instructorstudent communication. See Tables 2 and 3 for examples of student comments.

#### Discussion

HyFlex learning promotes student-centered instruction that is equitable, accessible, and flexible and provides nurse educators with an opportunity to rethink and imagine the future of higher education. A substantial advantage of the HyFlex model is its ability to be adaptive during unprecedented times, which, as we know from the COVID-19 pandemic and other crises, can occur at any given time. By simultaneously using face-to-face instruction, online live streaming, and online asynchronous delivery, students can more seamlessly move from face-to-face to online formats. This research provides valuable insight into the process of designing HyFlex courses and student perceptions after engaging in HyFlex modalities.

#### Faculty Reflections

Faculty met the challenge of instituting HyFlex courses and developing a more studentcentered learning environment. Faculty avoided monotonous teaching strategies and gave equal attention to both online and in-classroom learners. The ability to provide access to peer interaction ensured that the learners' experiences were enriched through synchronous communication with their peers. Further research is needed to assess how this approach reduces the sense of isolation among students.

Faculty reflections also illustrate how implementing HyFlex requires robust support and collaboration from the institution. In particular, support from the Office of Information Technology (OIT), the library, the writing center, and student support services. The university has to adapt its services to the HyFlex model to ensure equity in access regardless of course modality. While teaching HyFlex courses, faculty proactively sought opportunities to learn new technology-related skills, provide learning support, and improve instructional design. Resources are needed to ensure faculty can access the professional development needed to support their growth in this area.

#### Student Reflections and Experiences

HyFlex modalities helped faculty in the UHCL RN-BSN foster a student-centered learning environment. Emergent themes from the questionnaire show that nursing students felt HyFlex afforded them flexibility in scheduling and facilitated better school-work-life balance. Students also conveyed the need for technological resources, including internet access and strong instructorstudent communication, to improve the HyFlex course experience. The findings related to students' ability to further their education while maintaining a work-life balance is validation that using the HyFlex modality may assist in helping RNs further their education and, ultimately, contribute to addressing the nursing shortage.

#### Considerations for Nursing Student Success

This research provides multiple considerations for how HyFlex can improve outcomes for nursing students. Based on their experiences with the HyFlex pilot, faculty recommend that students use university resources, including those offered by OIT and the Department of Nursing, to strengthen their ability to take courses in their modality of choice. Faculty noted the connection between students' skill levels in using technology and their ability to make legitimate choices about participation modes. Students who desire to engage in the online options should also consult with their respective internet providers to ensure they have appropriate connectivity and identify resources or discounted student services. Furthermore, academic programs may want to consider providing resources and extra training to students (and instructors) so that flexible participation is a real option.

To be successful in HyFlex courses, students must gain a solid understanding of the course rubrics, calendars, and reminders for assessments due at the start of the course. Moreover, students must seek out and utilize opportunities to support their learning needs, such as those provided through the course,

seminars, and workshops offered by the institution. Learners should also take the initiative to actively engage in classroom activities to maintain sufficient communication and interaction with their peers.

The HyFlex modality is currently in place and is the only degree model offered in the UHCL nursing program. Departmental data analysis shows that HyFlex has increased course completion and program graduation rates. Pilot participants completed all courses, and most students could graduate by completing two-three semesters of coursework full-time instead of pursuing the parttime option (four-six semesters). Additional research is needed to examine how HyFlex contributes to lower attrition rates, as well as increased persistence, retention, and completion.

#### Implications for Nursing Education and Practice

Results from the 2022 National Nursing Workforce Survey suggests that the percentage of RNs with a BSN or higher degree in the US workforce exceeded 70% for the first time (71.7%) (NCSBN, 2023; US Bureau of Labor Statistics, 2022). Although most nurses now enter the workforce with a BSN or graduate degree, we must continue to find ways to boost BSN program enrollment and make nursing education more flexible.

For more than two decades, policymakers, healthcare authorities, and practice leaders have recognized that advancements in nursing education can directly impact nursing practice and help strengthen the nursing workforce (AACN, 2023). Investments in nursing education are needed to address the nursing shortage and increase access and opportunities within BSN programs. As educators, we are responsible for finding ways to rethink education educational opportunities and evolve course delivery continuously. It is imperative to build an education system and culture that promotes and supports lifelong learning that is accessible throughout a nurse's working life.

#### Study Limitations

Though this research does not seek to be generalizable, the findings should be considered in context with the setting and sample size. The questionnaire contained four questions and was submitted by less than 30 RN-BSN students. Additionally, due to the shorter length of the program (two to three semesters), most of the first-year graduates were not included in the sample.

## Voices of Resilience: An Autoethnographic Exploration of the Contributions of Black Nursing Faculty in Nursing Education

#### Abstract

Background: Historically, racial disparities plague the U.S. nursing profession. The lack of racial diversity in academia leads to low nursing student enrollment, insufficient racial/ethnic diversity in the nursing workforce, and disparate healthcare access and outcomes, subsequently increasing morbidity and mortality for millions of U.S residents. Although it is known that negative interpersonal experiences influence retention of Black leaders in academia, few studies have explored the lived experience of Black academic nurses in their daily work as faculty members.

Purpose: The objective of this study was to capture the lived experience of Black academic nurses to elucidate their experiences navigating nursing academia.

Method: The researchers employed an autoethnographic qualitative study of faculty and academic leaders to examine their context critically. Participants held a Ph.D. or DNP from diverse institutions, ranks, and tenure, with 2 to 35 academic years.

Findings: The inductive process resulted in four major themes: mentoring, a culture of oppression, a sense of duty, and breaking the cycle to build a legacy accompanied by thirteen sub-themes reported.

Conclusion: Understanding the lived experience of this subset of nurses can be used to help guide strategic optimization of work culture. This study uniquely contributes to diversity, equity, and inclusivity science.

Keywords: academic nursing, Black nurse faculty, Association of Black Nursing Faculty, autoethnography, diversity, DEIB.

#### Introduction

The nursing profession has a long-standing history of racial disparities, but it has also witnessed Black nurses' resilience and unwavering commitment, including that of Black nurse faculty. The lack of racial diversity in faculty directly impacts the nursing profession's need to attract diverse nursing students (Montgomery et al., 2021) to diversify the nursing workforce for more equitable health outcomes. Further, it is paralleled by recruiting more faculty from minority populations (American Association of Colleges of Nursing (AACN), 2022). Few nurses from racial/ethnic minority groups with advanced nursing degrees pursue faculty careers. According to 2021 data from AACN's annual survey, approximately 19% of fulltime nursing school faculty come from racial minority backgrounds (AACN, 2022). According to the 2020 U.S. census, the population comprised Whites alone at 75.5%, Black or African Americans at 13.6%, American Indian and Alaska Native at 1.3%, Asians at 6.3%, Native Hawaiian and Other Pacific Islanders at 0.3%, and two or more races at 3.0%. In this paper, the concept of race is separate from the concept of Hispanic origin (U.S. Census Bureau, 2020). Hispanics or Latinos comprise 19.1%, and whites alone, non-Hispanics, or Latinos comprise 58.9% (U.S. Census Bureau, 2020).

While the United States (U.S.) population is more diverse than ever, the nursing profession does not reflect this diversity. It remains overwhelmingly White (80%) as compared to Black (6.3%), Asian (7.4%), two or more races (2.5%), Native American or Alaska Native (0.4%), Native Hawaiian or other Pacific Islander (0.4) (Smiley et al., 2021). Of all the registered nurse (RN) workforce, 6.9% report their

ethnicity as Hispanic (Smiley et al., 2021). The deficiency of racial diversity in academic nursing can bring about feelings of isolation, conflict, and lack of community for Black faculty and students (Montgomery, 2021). This paper focuses on race identification rather than ethnicity. Therefore, the paper reflects the narratives of nursing faculty who racially identify as Black without regard for their ethnic classification as Hispanic or non-Hispanic.

#### Significance of Black Nurse Faculty Contributions

The contributions of Black nursing faculty to nursing and nursing education are significant, although often under-reported. A diverse nursing faculty helps to recruit and retain a more diverse student population through role modeling (AACN, 2024; Ganek et al., 2023). The presence of racially diverse faculty in academic nursing conveys to racially diverse students the possibility of becoming RNs and nurse educators in academia or the clinical setting (Iheduru-Anderson, 2020). On the other hand, the absence of racial minority nurse educators may send a message to potential students that nursing does not value diversity or offers career advancement opportunities within the profession. Further, when students seek academic role models to encourage and enhance their learning, the lack of diverse role models may lead to frustration in their attempts to find mentors (AACN, 2022). Research indicates that having ethnically and racially diverse nursing faculty can contribute to recruiting, retaining, and graduating diverse students reflective of the U.S. population (AACN, 2017; Brooks et al., 2022; Ganek et al., 2023). The Future of Nursing 2020–2030 Charting a Path to Achieve Health Equity Report recommends that nursing education re-evaluate hiring practices to achieve a more diverse faculty workforce (National Academies of Medicine, Engineering, and Medicine, 2021).

Given the critical need for a diverse nurse educator workforce, specifically Black faculty, this qualitative study using an autoethnography approach is an explorative journey into the lived experiences of Black nursing faculty who are active members of the Association of Black Nursing Faculty Inc (ABNF), employed in academic nursing programs as faculty, and currently holding academic nursing leadership positions. This autoethnographic endeavor aims to amplify the often marginalized voices through reflective narratives of their struggles, triumphs, and

resilient spirits. Through a profoundly introspective lens, the authors reflect on their journey in academic nursing, situating their experiences within the greater social context of an ever-evolving higher education landscape. In this narrative exploration, we aim to amplify the voices of resilience, shedding light on the Black nursing faculty's invaluable role in shaping the current and future of nursing practice and education through the Black nurses' storied contributions.

#### Purpose of the Paper

The purpose of this paper is 1) to highlight the experience and perspectives of Black nurse faculty and 2) to pinpoint the critical role of Black nurse faculty in fostering diversity, equity, inclusion, and belonging (DEIB) in nursing education. Based on this, we will delve into the multifaceted contributions of Black nurse faculty members to nursing education from the perspectives of selfselected members of the ABNF. The research question guiding this study is: How do Black nursing faculty experience and navigate nursing academia?

#### Philosophical and Theoretical Perspectives

We approach this study from a constructivist perspective, which posits that reality is socially constructed and knowledge is created through interaction between individuals and their environment (Crotty, 1998). Further, new knowledge is built from existing knowledge gleaned from the context of real-world experience (Sukumar & Metoyer, 2019). Firmly rooted in constructivism, this paper embarks on a journey to illuminate the personal narratives of self-selected Black nursing faculty to shed light on their experiences and the profound impact of their contribution to nursing education. By telling their story through the prism of their voices, we gain insights into the complexities of race, identity, and the indomitable spirit that fuels their dedication to fostering the next generation of nursing professionals. Through storytelling, we seek "to challenge the oppressive structures that create the conditions for silencing" (Tierney, 1998, p. 55).

#### Theoretical Framework

#### *Resilience Theory*

Resilience theory is a framework for studying adversity and how adverse life experiences impact

people (Van Breda, 2018). According to van Breda (2018, p. 1), resilience theory is the study of the things (robust social support networks, practical problem-solving skills, a keen sense of self-worth, and a clear sense of purpose) that make the phenomenon whole. Conversely, it also considers risk factors that can increase vulnerability to adverse events. At its core, resilience theory emphasizes that resilience is not a fixed trait, but a dynamic process influenced by many factors. Resilience varies from person to person and across different life stages, depending on an individual's unique characteristics and life experiences (Foster et al., 2019; Van Breda, 2018). At its core, resilience means enduring significant disruptions or stress without lasting change, disintegration, or permanent damage. It is the ability to withstand, recover from, and regroup after adversity and exhibit minimal distortion.

Resilience theory was selected for this study to allow Black nursing faculty, whose voices are often underrepresented, to illuminate their lived experience within the predominance of whiteness in nursing, specifically in academic settings. The underrepresentation of Black people in academic nursing means they have to navigate many circumstances often described as adversarial (Beard & Julion, 2016; Iheduru-Anderson, 2021; Iheduru-Anderson & Shingles, 2023). This article examines Black nursing faculty's strategies and coping mechanisms in response to challenges and adversity within academia (Fisher & Jones, n.d.; Hart et al., 2016).

The resilience theory in this current article provides the framework to explore the process of not overcoming adversity but instead using agency to change the odds by challenging adversities (Fisher & Jones, n.d.; Hart et al., 2016), specifically inherent in academic nursing settings. The aspect of resiliency theory, which supports the neo-liberalization of resilience (placing the responsibility for personal growth and development solely on individuals, reducing state support, and dismantling the welfare system) (Garrett, 2016), is not a focus of this current study. In contrast, resilience theory in this article serves as a framework to explore the process of not overcoming adversity but of using agency to change the odds by challenging the adversities (Fisher & Jones, n.d.; Hart et al., 2016) inherent in academic nursing.

#### Literature Review

The lack of Black nurses in the U.S. is not new. However, Black nurses have been at the margins of nursing historiography, and there is a scarcity of historical accounts highlighting Black nurse pioneers and their nursing work (National Commission to Address Racism in Nursing, 2022). Research has shown that Black faculty are often "forced to invalidate stereotypes and legitimize [our]competence, intelligence, and overall worth" (Bertrand Jones et al., 2013, p. 326). In contrast, despite stereotypes, Black people, specifically Black women, persist through stereotypes and earn degrees at high rates; our stories are often excluded from the success discourse (Patton & Croom, 2017). Throughout the 19th century and first half of the 20th, Black students, nurses, and educators fought for equality (Montgomery et al., 2021). Moreover, historically, Black colleges and universities such as Historically Black Universities (HBCUs) have also been critical to educating Black nurses. Good examples were Hampton University, offering the first masters and doctoral programs for Black nurses, and Florida Agricultural and Mechanical University's 85 year-old BSN program, the longest continually running BSN program offered for Black nurses. These were accomplished from the work set forth by these Black nurse pioneers (Montgomery et al., 2021). Harriet Tubman is known for her heroic work during the U.S. Civil War. Several Black pioneers of nursing education have displayed courage and perseverance in the ongoing face of hostile political and social climate post-slavery and "Jim Crow laws" and include the efforts of Dr. Sallie Allen Tucker, the founder of the ABNF, Inc.; Goldie D. Brangman-Dumpson, Co-Founder of the Harlem Hospital School for Nurse Anesthetists, and the first Black President of the American Association of Nurse Anesthetists (AANA), serving from 1973 to 1974; and Estelle Massey Riddle Osborne, the first Black nurse to earn a master's degree (Montgomery et al., 2021). A few of the unsung Black nurse trailblazers are outlined below.

Harriet Tubman was a nurse innovator who served in the Northern Army, later cared for family members, and continued her humanitarian work for those needing shelter, food, clothing, and medical attention (Larson, 2014; Hobson, 2024). At the age of 74, Tubman purchased 25 acres, and her vision of creating a nursing home for the poor and older adults in her community was realized with the creation of the Tubman Home for Aged and Indigent Negroes, which opened in 1908 with the support of the African Methodist Episcopal Zion Church (Hobson, 2024; National Park Service, 2023).

Dr. Tucker's "Gem of an Idea" led to the founding of ABNF Inc. on March 1, 1987 (ABNF.net, 2023). The ABNF is an association dedicated to promoting the work of nurse scholars who share similar credentials, interests, and concerns, working to promote health-related issues and educational interests to benefit themselves and the Black community. Dr. Allen-Tucker has modeled and been featured as the ideal mentor to Black nurse academics (Johnson et al., 2023). The ABNF has supported the development and promotion of Black Nursing faculty members and leaders through peer mentoring, scholarships, scientific publications, advancement in nursing education, and helping shape nursing education and practice. To address the publishing gap of research produced by the Nurse Faculty of Color, Dr. Tucker founded the publishing house–Tucker Publications, Inc., which helped magnify the voice of Black nursing faculty by disseminating their work in books and peer-reviewed journals (Johnson et al., 2023).

Goldie D. Brangman-Dumpson, born in 1917, graduated from Harlem Hospital Center's nursing program after volunteering with the Red Cross. She graduated in 1943 and accepted her first job at Harlem Hospital. Goldie had an opportunity to become a nurse anesthetist in 1949. She was instrumental in starting the Nurse Anesthetists program at Harlem Hospital, which accepted Black nurses who were excluded from other anesthesia schools. Goldie also received her Master of Business Administration and a Master of Nursing Education. Despite the ongoing challenges as a Black nurse educator, she worked at Harlem Hospital for another 45 years. She directed the Harlem Hospital School of Nurse Anesthesia for over 30 years, educating over 700 students, many of whom were Black nurses. She also became the first — and so far, only — Black President of the American Association of Nurse Anesthetists (AANA), serving from 1973 to 1974 (Nurses You Should Know, 2022).

Estelle Massey Osborne is a trailblazing figure in nursing history as the first Black nurse to attain a master's degree, with her achievements

documented by NYU Rory Meyer College of Nursing (NYU, 2020). In addition to this milestone, Osborne was the first African American instructor at the Harlem Hospital School of Nursing. She contributed as a consultant to the National Nursing Council for War Service during World War II, advocating for removing discriminatory policies in nursing schools (NYU, 2020). Her influential tenure as president of the National Association of Colored Graduate Nurses (NACGN) from 1934 to 1939 was characterized by a commitment to advancing the professionalization, education, and practice opportunities for Black nurses nationwide (NYU, 2020). Breaking new ground in 1940, Osborne became the first Black Superintendent of Nurses at St. Louis's Homer G. Phillips Hospital, establishing connections with key nursing organizations. Further contributions included her memberships in the National Urban League and as the first vice-president of the National Council of Negro Women, reflecting a dedication to improving health education and services for rural Black communities (NYU, 2020). Osborne's multifaceted career, marked by numerous firsts, underscores her commitment to dismantling barriers and advancing the nursing profession.

It is evident in the literature that the dearth of Black women in academia working in historically and predominantly White colleges and universities intensifies feelings of entrapment and silencing (Sue et al., 2007; 2019). Black women in academia experience ongoing microaggressions--characterized by subtle indignities that communicate insults to minoritized and marginalized individuals (Sue et al., 2019). Although some literature regarding microaggression alludes to its undesirable impact on students' success in nursing academia, very few studies include Black faculty experiences. The current study aims to fill the gap in the literature by exploring how Black nursing faculty navigate nursing academia and their contributions to fostering diversity and inclusion in the nursing setting.

#### Methods

#### Design

The current study employs an autoethnographic qualitative method to explore the experience of Black nursing faculty. Autoethnography is often used to explore and understand complex and personal issues related to

identity, culture, and social phenomena. According to Keles (2022, p. 2027), "autoethnography aims to better understand how cultural discourses shape and are shaped by individuals who inhabit, negotiate with, accept, and resist their immediate social groups." Autoethnography is a research approach that allows individuals to narrate their experiences and critically examine their cultural context. Autoethnography is appropriate for this paper, as it enables us to center our voices and unique stories and perspectives within academic nursing. The current design was selected because "autoethnography confronts dominant forms of representation and power in an attempt to reclaim, through selfreflective response, representational spaces that have marginalized those of us at the borders" (Tierney, 1998, p. 66). According to Creswell (2013, p. 73), the focus on cultural meaning inherent in autoethnographic narratives, is that it "contains the personal story of the author as well as the larger cultural meaning for the individual's story." Although there is debate about autoethnography as a sound research method, it offers a more accurate and credible account of firsthand experiences, given its insider's perspective of the auto ethnographer.

In this paper, we use collaborative autoethnography (CAE) as a research design. CAE is a qualitative research design where a group of researchers collaborate as research informants to produce personal accounts of their experiences (Lapadat, 2017) related to a specific context. They explore self in a community instead of in solitude. Through collaborative work, they explore similarities and differences in their experiences. It is a collaborative reflective experience where individuals must feel comfortable engaging in emotional selfreflexivity (Chang et al., 2016). CAE, a decolonizing qualitative research approach, fosters supportive communities for personal and professional growth (Thambinathan & Kinsella, 2021). It empowers marginalized voices through social inquiry, challenging positivistic notions of research significance. CAE encourages collaborative authorship throughout the research process. We aim to examine and reflect on each participant's personal experiences and place those experiences within a broader cultural and societal context (Keles, 2022) of academic nursing. The CAE process is iterative (Chang et al., 2016; Long & Travers, 2021), consisting of four steps, as detailed in Figure 1.

#### Ethical Considerations

The permission for the current study was obtained through the University of Houston-Clear Lake Institutional Review Board, in reliance with the respective universities and colleges of the researchers. For confidentiality, the authors assumed pseudonyms for their narratives. The lead author then changed the pseudonyms before assigning the narrative for initial coding to the authors to enhance the confidentiality of the research team. Using gender-neutral pronouns and reporting the findings using pseudonyms and gender-neutral pronouns limits the connection of the experiences to a single individual. It promotes the confidentiality of shared experiences. Sharing deeply personal experiences makes the ethnographer vulnerable, with possible undesired career consequences, the extent of which could not be determined for the future. Therefore, all authors were encouraged to reflect on their experience and self-determine what they were comfortable sharing without pressure. The goal is not to generalize our findings, although readers may connect our shared experiences with their own social or cultural context.

#### Sampling

The authors are nursing faculty members of the Association of Black Nursing Faculty (ABNF) Inc. In CAE, the researchers are the active instruments and participants building on each other's stories to construct meanings of their personal experiences within their sociocultural contexts (Chang et al., 2012). In collaborative autoethnography, a group of researchers pool their stories together around a specific context. Therefore, the sampling method is purposeful and specific to members of a group who share common characteristics and are willing to engage in selfinterrogation and reflexivity on an agreed topic within a specific context (Chang et al., 2016). In this study, the common characteristics and inclusion criteria is that ethnographers must identify as Black, be current nurse educators, have terminal degrees and be active members of ABNF. It is a self-selected group of Black nurse academics working in various academic settings across the U.S. Excluded were

those not self-identifying as Black and currently not working in an academic setting.

All study participants self-identified as female, although males and females were invited to participate. Sample size for collaborative autoethnography can range from two to 15 (Chang et al., 2012). Therefore, the team discussed the idea for the study during one of their monthly scheduled meetings, and those interested in participating were invited to form the collaborative writing group. The researchers met regularly to discuss topics of interest, the data collection process, and maintaining the confidentiality of the information shared during the research process. #### Data Collection

In autoethnography, the self is the object of the study (Richards, 2008). Data collection in autoethnographic research involves personal narratives, stories, or accounts. The researchers, as the object of the study, are deeply embedded within the nursing academic community, culture, and knowledge production. Storytelling in the form of a narrative grounded in the researchers' personal experiences provides nuanced insight into a phenomenon. In autoethnography, the researchers' individual experiences are the central subject of the data. By writing about oneself in autoethnography, the individual takes control of curating their own story and resists "being objectified by others" (Richards, 2008, p. 4). Researchers use this approach to illustrate being a part of a larger sociocultural context and meticulously capture the intricate nuances of individual lived experiences (Keles, 2022; Richards, 2008). Researchers may write autobiographical narratives that describe their experiences in detail, including emotions, thoughts, and sensory perceptions. The centrality of the insider's voice is crucial for creating authentic and compelling narratives. The authenticity of the researchers' voice is critical to autoethnography. It allows for including personal anecdotes, emotions, and cultural nuances that make stories more engaging and relatable (Cooper & Lilyea, 2022; Keles, 2022; Richards, 2008). Researchers aim to present their experiences honestly and in their own voice without overly academic or detached language.

For this study, the authors provided selfreflective written narratives of their experiences as Black nurse educators in response to three guiding questions in Table 1. This critical reflection explains participants' internal dialogues and analyzes their thought processes. Further, oral, or written reflections are a well-documented data collection method widely used in qualitative research, particularly in educational research (Mafinejad et al., 2022). Authors create structured questions to guide the self-reflective data collection (Cooper & Lilyea, 2022; Keles, 2022; Lai, 2012). The authors constructed the three guiding questions in Table 1 during their second meeting about the study to assist the authors in focusing their reflective narrative journals on answering the research question. The authors were instructed to reflect on their individual experiences, thoughts, feelings, and memories related to their lived experiences as Black academics in nursing programs. They each wrote narratives or journal entries that captured these personal insights. There was no prescribed method or areas or experiences that individuals were required to share.

#### Trustworthiness

Data trustworthiness was supported by efforts to reduce bias involving reflexivity and intersubjectivity. Reflexivity is a salient component in autoethnography that entails a process of selfreference (Davies, 1999). Researchers must acknowledge the subjectivity of their experiences, consistently reflect on their perspectives and biases, and how their identity and positionality influence the interpretations of their lived experiences (Darwin Holmes, 2020). The researcher must be critical of one's self-awareness to reduce bias (Poerwandari, 2021). Intersubjectivity is the interchange of thoughts and feelings between individuals, resulting in increased awareness of others' intentions and feelings and the self via dynamic sharing of the minds, companionship, and exchange of emotional evaluations and self-conscious intentions (Kokkinaki et al., 2023). Reflexivity and dialogue intersubjectivity occurred during frequent research meetings that allowed for member checks and dialogue (Noble et al., 2021; Poerwandari, 2021). The authors met regularly to discuss progress, data collection, and analysis. Between meetings, the lead author communicated with the group to review, comment, and address concerns relevant to the

project. During these meetings, discussions of the narratives and critical self-reflection occurred as members shared ideas and realizations and clarified the meaning of narratives. Honesty was supported through dialogue that encouraged self-awareness, acceptance of experiences, and expression of these experiences among participants for critical selfreflection, feedback, and clarity of interpretations (Kokkinaki et al., 2023; Noble et al., 2021; Poerwandari, 2021).

#### Data Analysis

Inductive data analysis described by Braun and Clarke (2006; 2012) was performed for the reflective narratives of participants. The analysis was a collaborative effort of all contributors. All the authors were encouraged to read each narrative after submitting them in a shared Google Drive accessible only to the team members. All members of the team performed the initial coding of the data. The lead author assigned deidentified narrative transcripts to each author to complete the initial coding (Braun & Clarke, 2012). Following the initial coding, a group of qualitative research experts in the team volunteered to complete the rest of the analysis, as described by Braun and Clarke (2006; 2012). The formation of the analysis group was for efficiency and timely completion of the analysis process. The group worked individually and collectively to complete the analysis over a month. The final themes and sub-themes (See Figure 2) were constructed during the last data analysis meeting.

 During the data analysis, each researcher maintained a reflexive journal to record their thoughts and reflections throughout the research process. The themes recorded were shared with the research team to encourage discussions and reflections during the meetings. The reflective journals helped us to critically analyze our perceptions, thoughts, biases, and interpretations of the data. Once the thematic map was created and finalized, the team worked separately and met every two weeks to discuss the findings and write the report. All research team members are doctoralprepared and have unrestricted editing access to the projected documents. There were ongoing revisions based on the contributions of the team.

#### Findings

Ten Black nursing faculty and leaders

contributed to the current study. All study participants self-identified as women, although all people were invited to participate. CAE has the potential to violate participants and acquaintance confidentiality. In an effort to protect the confidentiality of the participants, only aggregate data about the participants' demographics are reported. The region of the country and institutions of employment are not reported. The participants are all women. All participants held doctoral degrees (PhD or DNP). The number of years in nursing ranged from 20 to 42 years, and their years in academic nursing span between 2 to 35 years. Four participants reported being in nontenure track positions; six reported being in tenure track positions; four are currently tenured, and the other two are working toward tenure. The faculty ranks included one full professor, five associate professors, two assistant professors, and two without faculty ranks. In the current study, faculty represent state and private academic institutions in various regions of the U.S. The demographic information for participants is presented in Table 2.

The majority of participants began their narratives with the following sentence or something similar to convey that their experiences as nurse academics cannot be limited to the short narrative provided to answer the research questions:

"The narratives shared here are by no means near all that I have encountered, endured, and overcome as a Black nursing faculty. However, the narratives provide insight into my perspectives of the experiences that shaped my life in academic nursing and how I remember, feel, and interpret them as I reflect on those experiences at this time."

Participants discussed how they navigated nursing academia, "the defining experiences, their challenges, triumphs and their unwavering need for grace under fire."

The study findings are described under four major categories/themes and thirteen subthemes. The four emerging themes were mentoring, a culture of oppression, a sense of duty (fighting the good fight) and breaking the cycle to build a legacy. although the themes are distinctive, they are not discrete; there is some overlap among them. The themes and subthemes are discussed in greater detail in the following section. With resilience theory guiding this study, resilience was the adopted coping mechanism used in response to adversity in nursing academia, such as limited mentoring, the anticipated need to fight the good fight, a culture of oppression, and the need for change to break the cycle and create opportunities for success.

#### Mentoring

Mentoring is critical for career development, transition to the academic setting, and persistence. All the participants discussed in great detail their mentoring experiences or lack thereof. They also discussed the need to be mentored by someone from a similar racial, ethnic, or cultural background. Participants shared how it is important to mentor and be mentored. Many of them were mentored by people from diverse backgrounds. Some wished to have the opportunity to be mentored by someone who looks like them, believing it would have made a difference early in their careers.

All participants viewed working in academia as essential for increasing the diversity of the nursing workforce and creating a sense of belonging for the underrepresented students of color. Being a role model for the few Black students is one of the most cited reasons for persistence in the academy. They felt the need to convert the "myth of diversity to a reality" and break the cycle of marginalization and underrepresentation of Black people in nursing. Mentoring is discussed under two sub-themes: affinity mentoring and loss of support and resources.

#### Affinity Mentoring

Affinity mentoring is self-explanatory. The participants want to have someone like them help support their growth in the academy. The underrepresentation of Black nurses in the academy makes this challenging. Therefore, the participants view it as their responsibility to mentor those like them, including the students. Some participants discussed finding mentors from ethnic nursing organizations. For example, Imani stated in their narrative, "Despite the challenges, the mentorship I received came from the trailblazers I connected to through my membership in ABNF." Bakari expressed the power and comfort inherent in affinity mentoring. "When you have someone who speaks your language, who understands the subtle but shared language, they need no long-winded explanation of what you are trying to convey. You can feel comfortable in your skin." Adina added, "Students and new nurses want to believe that there is an opportunity for growth in the profession. Having someone like them in this position makes them believe it is possible." Just being present in the classroom or clinical setting is a sign of hope.

The participants shared that they felt they received clear messages about expectations in academic nursing from other faculty of color. Viola noted, "While her comment was not meant to discourage, it painted a clear picture of what I can expect from the institution as a woman of color. There were no mincing words." The presence of other Black faculty or leaders in the institutions provided a type of psychological anchor for the participants. It feels good not to be the only one, even when the person is not a faculty member or a leader. Being mentored by someone from a similar background helps affirm individual and shared cultural identities. This is significant for retaining persons of color in predominantly white environments. The mentor helps you feel seen. The hunger for affinity groups contributed to the need to create their own support community, further discussed in the subtheme Creating my own Village.

#### Loss of Support or Resources

In describing mentoring and support for the self and others in academia, many participants described experiences of working with minimal support or receiving support from their superiors and peers and losing the support for no discernible reason shortly after recruitment or within a few months or years. The loss of support sometimes coincides with the participant achieving a milestone or getting recognition for their work. Sometimes, the loss of support resulted from speaking up and standing up for themselves or others. The loss of support, which results when the participants speak up for themselves or challenge the status quo, served to maintain the culture of silence, perpetuating tokenism, and isolation. The study participants shared experiences that led to a sense of discrimination. These experiences lend themselves to the expression, "I am guilty of being Black." Imani explained, "Being Black, you are not included in the conversations or accepted in the "clicks" that are established with the

other than Black faculty." Viola described experiencing discrimination, sharing, "I have been discriminated against for just being my authentic self and doing my job."

This perception of lack of support also influenced the work environment. Viola described the work environment as "overwhelming and challenging" due to being one of the few Black nursing faculty members within the institution. Ekene explained this.

> "I initially had tremendous support from my dean and executive vice dean. After a while, they withdrew much of their open support for me. I began to lose out completely on their support and felt alone and isolated at times."

Another participant shared a similar experience.

 "I gradually felt the support being withdrawn like I was undeserving of their support. I found myself doing more with less support and resources and no plans to provide support. When I ask for help with charts, numbers, and so on. I did not get help. They felt that I could do much more with much less." [Aidina]

Pearl was more forgiving when reflecting on the lack of support in their leadership role.

> "The resources were not enough, but I made do with what I was given, and my mindset was, I would be given the resources when they could afford to do so."

These reflections give credence to the rampant lack of support as revealed by the participant experiences.

#### A Culture of Oppression

Black nurses have been historically excluded from participating in nursing and academia. The culture of oppression limits opportunity for these participants. Even in their roles as faculty and leaders, they continue to experience oppression, from silencing to a lack of trust in various dimensions. Their underrepresentation in academia makes them highly visible, creating room for hyper-scrutiny of their work, behavior, and attitude. The hyper scrutiny and the hypervisibility make the participant vigilant, making it difficult to trust. The lack of trust does not always result from the hyper scrutiny but from feeling betrayed by those they thought they could

trust. The culture of oppression is discussed under the subthemes: a culture of silence, forced silence, trust/breach of trust, and hypervisibility. Some participants' presence in academic leadership was questioned, and their credibility was undermined; "How did you get here? Camara, who is fairly new to nursing academia, described it as "cutthroat."

 Cámara shared being questioned about her qualifications and credentials. "What qualifications do you have that qualify you for this position? And some of the personal attacks experienced from peers while in her position. Additionally, Cámara shared that a few individuals were displeased with her academic appointment. "They made it a personal mission to undermine and attempt to sabotage my credibility and contributions at every turn." How Black and White faculty experience and manage the culture of nursing academia is evident in Ekene's narrative.

> A colleague asked me what my routine is when I get home, and I told her, 'Well, I take about an hour or two to process through the daily micro and macro aggressions that I had to either ignore, address, or compartmentalize from the day…. I sit and do affirmations to prevent myself from internalizing it as having anything to do with me or my contributions.' her face is fraught with disdain, disbelief, and shock. She replied, I go home, have a glass of red wine, and to a home-cooked meal by my partner, and we revel in the ending of another successful day at the office.' I replied, 'Wow, that sounds really nice; what a privilege you get to do that when you get home. [Ekene]

These reflections support microaggressions that function insidiously in ways that emotionally exhaust and serve to undermine one's sense of place, belonging, and self.

#### A Culture of Silence

Participants described a culture of silence where, individually, and collectively, people fail to speak up in the face of injustices and inequalities. Due to underrepresentation in academia, Black nursing faculty are often exposed to a work environment that leaves the impression that

appeasing their colleagues, including leadership, is necessary. As a result, the individual's needs go unidentified and unmet. Work environments like these lack the necessary systems to promote trust and increase feelings of invisibility. The participants expressed their displeasure with the silence from their colleagues even when they witnessed explicit acts of racism or bias. Aidina explained in the following statement. "It was always disheartening when other faculty who know very well that a faculty's actions are racially motivated, an act of explicit bias, and they say nothing." Imani contemplated the reason for the ongoing silence by nurse academics. "Why are those in positions to speak up for those who cannot keep quiet? What do they stand to gain by keeping quiet? How can they keep quiet after posting the equity and equal opportunity statement on the website?" Participants went on to say that it is up to Black nursing academics and other academics of color to work to retain students of color to increase their representation in the nursing profession.

Within this culture of silence is a culture of exclusion. "There is a feeling that we should be seen, not heard," said Imani. Ekene's colleagues even shared that they felt like Ekene did not belong in the department. They were conveying the message that they were a token hire. They took particular care to analyze Ekene's CV, questioning the validity of the accomplishments detailed in the document. They also inquired about how someone so young and Black can accomplish so much to be considered for the position they are in. They reinforced their sentiment about Ekene's place in the institution by questioning every decision and refusing to report to or work with them. Two White women in Ekene's team said they felt excluded because they were hiring diverse staff (many people who are not white) for the department, placing them in the minority.

#### Forced Silence

Participants described experiences of forced silence created by an unwelcoming environment. Participants felt that speaking up was detrimental to their jobs or positions. Those early in their career, nontenured or in nontenure track positions, felt the need to keep quiet to keep their jobs. This forced silence made participants feel they lacked a voice and agency. Participants discussed how forced silence left them feeling under-respected and lacking power

and credibility. Participants also talked about keeping quiet to get along or not to be labeled as troublemakers or poor team players. "I felt used and abused in many positions, and I wonder if it is my inability to speak up," said Sonia of her experience in nursing academia. Sonia elaborated on the comment, saying,

> I am told that I am not a team player when I speak up. My assertive, confident demeanor and attitude toward education, my success, and my hard work were perceived as either a threat or as bragging. They wanted the Black success story to be quiet and humble. Where other people's successes are celebrated, mine is brushed off as inconsequential, not good enough to be celebrated. [Sonia]

Trust/Breach of Trust

Participants shared concerns about lack of trust within their institutions, including experiences of betrayal of trust by individuals they felt they could confide in. Due to these trust issues, participants described how they frequently questioned others' intentions, making it increasingly difficult to find colleagues to confide in or solicit advice from without fear. The loss of trust in superiors and colleagues originated from the unfair treatment of some faculty and not others. As exemplified by Sonia's comment, "There is a sense of powerlessness, when Deans and department chairs are all the same race and have the same overtly racist behaviors and communication it is hard to know whom to trust."

Many of the participants discussed the lack of trust in their academic environment. Some participants felt "treated as a person with no credibility and power." [Opal] Aidina added to the sentiment about lack of trust in their abilities as educators and leaders, stating, "I feel deskilled like I am deliberately given assignments and responsibilities that require very little analytic thinking, sometimes more like an administrative assistant than a person who worked very hard to earn a doctoral degree." Sonia added, "I feel like I am just the paper pusher" when discussing decisionmaking power in their role as chair of a school committee. Participants shared the feeling that they have to constantly prove themselves when the same is not expected of their White peers. "You must always prove yourself capable and worthy of having

the same position as others who don't look like you," commented Imani. "I find myself trying to prove wrong those who think faculty of color are incapable, biased, and incompetent," commented Sonia. Participants felt that being treated differently and less than by your peers and superiors when you are hired for a position is a breach of trust. Bakari argued that it is management failure and a breach of trust when a person is hired for a position and not given the essential support to be successful. Opal expressed yearning for support from her department and not getting it. Viola explained that their "role was undermined and lacked support from both leadership and colleagues. I was not provided with adequate professional development and mentorship." This lack of trust leads some faculty to conclude that the institution makes efforts to prevent their long-term presence; as Sonia states, "mandates to hire diverse faculty are met, but faculty of color are undermined so that they will not stay."

Participants also discussed experiencing double standards in the way Black and White students are treated. Sonia commented, "I have witnessed double standards and unequal treatment involving faculty and students. where White students are given preferential treatment denied to others." Aidina elaborated on the unfair treatment of students of color.

> When I witness the differential treatment of students of color, that sends the message that they are not intelligent enough, when White faculty take extra time to review the student's paper because it is so well written, or the grade is 'much better than they expected of the Student of Color that it warrants extra scrutiny to make sure they did not cheat, it is a big problem. [Aidina]

The differential treatment of Black students in academic nursing is a cause for concern for these academics who understand the significance of increasing racial diversity in nursing for their communities. The underrepresentation of Black students and other students of color makes them stand out. It also makes it easy for them to be targeted for more scrutiny from predominantly White faculty who do not believe in their abilities to succeed. Excessive scrutiny of Black students and unfair treatment leads faculty to experience

uncertainty and wonder whether student evaluations of performance are conducted fairly; as Sonia stated regarding students of color, "I often wonder if they are being graded fairly."

#### Hypervisibility

While feeling invisible and viewed as an outsider were shared among participants, participants described experiencing increased scrutiny and feeling that eyes were always on them. Viola's comment exemplifies this. "You are highly visible… all eyes are on you and not always in a good way. You feel the need to double and triple check and even secondguess every act or decision you make." Due to increased scrutiny, participants shared that they were less outspoken and reserved and experienced fear in sharing their perspectives and opinions. Participants shared how they suffered in silence and yearned to find their own voice to speak up for themselves. One participant [Sonia] reported excessive scrutiny and attention involving comparison of her comments at meetings with those of another Black professor who frequently spoke up about injustices and unfair treatment and was told, "her comments would affect the perception of me and my tenure" and therefore she states, "I would alter my answers and not express how I truly feel." Excess scrutiny and attention lead to silence, diminishing the voices of faculty of color. One participant shared that an ideal work environment makes one feel "safe to voice my perspectives." These participants' reflections on being silenced illuminate the unrest or numbed response that can occur when one's expected place of belonging is threatened. They reported that as Black women in academia, there is no room for complacency; they must continuously be on guard and not show weakness. In doing so, they can care for themselves, safeguard their positions, and support their peers and students.

#### Sense of Duty (Fighting the Good Fight)

The participants see it as their responsibility to foster equity and inclusion to end the cycle of voices being underrepresented and excluded. It is important that all voices are heard within an institution in achieving a healthy work environment. This requires making Black nursing faculty feel welcome and removing situations/interactions where they think there is no time for them and that their opinions and thoughts do not count. Participants identified how a welcoming environment must

include being offered a seat at the table, recognizing their credibility, and creating a sense of belonging and collegiality. Participants' sense of duty is further described under the following subthemes: resilience, self-empowerment, creating my own village, sense of belonging, and I am more than enough.

#### Resilience

Participants deemed resilience a key characteristic in facing adversity at their institutions. Participants shared how resilience gave them strength. Cultivation of resilience provides many benefits. Participants discussed how resilience aided in finding their own voice, adopting emotional intelligence, and persevering as a leader and a mentor. While building resilience is a valuable strategy in the face of stressful situations and events, a supportive environment is required and should include the provision of adequate resources for success and instilling a sense of optimism.

Several comments by the participants exemplified their view about resilience in nursing academia. "I feel that I must persevere despite the odds. I do what I have to do. I stay strong and keep going," said Ekene. "I have to rely on my strong will and my leadership experience from the military to navigate nursing academia," added Imani. "As my mom used to say, don't let them see you cry," commented Bakari. "I am fighting the good fight that I hope will support behavior among all that promotes inclusion, respect, and success for all," Sonia said. Pearl's statement provided a good summary of the participants' resilience. "The more I felt I did not belong, the more self-empowered I was, especially realizing that the inequity in the practice setting could only be turned around if students were educated to deliver equitable, patient-centered care."

Spirituality and religion are an essential part of the participants' journey. Many find strength in their belief in a higher power. For example, Imani noted, "My biggest mentor is God, and my belief in God allows me to stand on Romans 8:31-39: 'If God is for me, who can be against me.'" Many engaged in daily self-reflection to weigh the pros and cons of enduring the challenges of working in nursing academia as Black women. "Every day, I find that I have to check in with myself and examine my boundaries and limits while facing the adversity I face in my daily work in nursing academia," Ekene commented. Many participants found that they

manage adversity better when they can depend on each other for support. Triumphing through adversity is critical for them as role models. They felt they must live above reproach and maintain high standards and expectations as role models. Ekene talked about using regular positive self-affirmation to fortify their resilience and teach their students to do the same.

#### Self-Empowerment

Some participants described feeling selfempowered by becoming emotionally smart to successfully "navigate the rough waters" in academia. Self-empowerment involves exercising one's autonomy freely. One participant shared that self-empowerment includes "modeling appropriate behavior and engagement, surrounding inclusion, scholarly discourse, healthy disagreement, policy change, and constructive criticism." Being selfempowered and empowering others is a vital strategy that should be used with one's adopted leadership style. Finding inner strength by not letting others see how their actions affect them was empowering for them. The participants felt their responsibility was to make a way where there was no way. Sonia gave an example of using personal and professional development opportunities to develop emotional intelligence to navigate difficult situations. Participants noted that sustainable practices such as emotional intelligence can be empowering. They observed, "After being thrown to the lions of formal academia, I quickly realized the need to develop different skills to succeed." Pearl added that a person has to become emotionally smart to survive nursing academia. For some participants, self-empowerment meant recognizing their worth and owning their place in the organization. Pearl's comment exemplifies this. "I had to believe I owned my place in the organization because I earned it. I refused to let anyone make me feel any different."

#### Creating my own Village

Participants described the need to find their village to thrive in academia. Ekene remarked the following about treatment received from colleagues, "they tried to carve out a circle of trusted individuals to help me navigate a system where it seemed to be incestuous and impenetrable." Participants emphasized the need for a supportive network to help self-evaluate and set meaningful goals. One

participant shared, "I seek out mentors outside of the organizations…I worked with great colleagues who were ready for my success." Within their institutions, participants found solidarity with other faculty members of color. Exemplified by Camara's comment. "The faculty of color in my school support each other in many ways because we understand our unique challenges in the space where we are." Camara went on to explain how they were supported by other faculty of color through their tenure process. "My colleagues of color, especially the Black ones, encouraged and supported me until I crossed the finish line." Sonia commented, "Faculty of Color, including myself, formed organizations and friendships to find support from within." Creating our village is done to have a safe space to reminisce and confer without judgment.

> "No one cares about the stress, sleepless nights, and the impact of these persistent adversities on health and well-being that we have to endure. No one gives us space to voice or tell these stories of challenges and triumphs within our institutions. We must find our own safe spaces and people to find solace and strength." [Ekene]

#### Sense of Belonging

In creating their own village, the participants created spaces and conditions where they could feel welcome, seen, and heard even when they were not with their immediate colleagues. They shared the need to reach out to colleagues from outside their departments or institutions for support.

"I began to feel isolated and confused...I have had to reach out to White and Black faculty from other institutions for support since faculty in my own institution perceived I was only there to meet the quota and hoped that I would not make it" [Sonia].

The participants felt it was their responsibility to make a way and create space for other people to feel like they belong.

#### I am More Than Enough

Several of the participants' reflections conveyed that they were excluded, made to feel less than, like they do not belong. This was conveyed through the participants' reflections when Camara spoke of an "Attack on cultural individual characteristics and appearance to discredit, undermine their intelligence and leadership abilities." Further, Adina exclaimed, "I feel deskilled. I am deliberately given assignments and responsibilities that require very little analytical thinking, sometimes more like an administrative assistant than someone who worked very hard to earn a doctoral degree." Another participant shared, "You must always prove yourself capable and worthy of having the same position as others who don't look like you…they sometimes feel you are aggressive or have an attitude that you think you are more than or too confident." Imani added to the sentiment, "It does not matter what they think of me; I am good enough." Despite these experiences, participants said they did not let the experiences eat away their selfesteem: "I am enough."

#### Breaking the Cycle to Build a Legacy

A driving force for the participants' persistence in academia is to break the cycle of underrepresentation and build a legacy that aims to sustain the recruitment and retention of Black nursing faculty. Participants discussed being the ones to advocate for students of color. "For my Black students, I work hard to undo the damage that may have been done to them in elementary and high school and even in college," said Aidina. Bakari added, "I persevere because of them. Someone did it for me, so I do it for them." For Viola, "When I remember the few Black students who believe in and rely on me as a role model, I continue to move forward." Victoria said, "We must remember those who paved the way for us to be in academia. You hang in there no matter what you experience so those who come after you will have a better experience." Sonia said, "The students of color particularly gravitate towards me for advice, support, and a listening ear…I am happy to serve in this capacity. I am happy to mentor these students." This theme is further discussed under the subthemes Finding My Voice and Ending the Diversity and Inclusion Myth.

#### Finding my voice

"Keeping quiet just to get along is no longer an option for me," said Imani. Pearl added, "I had the tenacity to find my voice, to speak up, and say

enough." Participants described how finding one's voice is critical. Finding their voice meant speaking up despite the personal and professional risks they may experience as a result. They speak up for themselves and their students. Adina noted, "As a Black faculty member, I have an added responsibility to stand up for these students and challenge other faculty members' biased assumptions about Students of Color while also fighting for my survival in nursing academia." They felt that to survive in academia, they needed to break the cycle of silence and stand up for themselves. Imani also explained that they need to be ready and willing to find another employment if their current employer insists on silencing their voices.

" There is a sense of powerlessness" [Opal] when you feel you do not have a voice. "Finding my voice was liberating. The trailblazers who came before me gave me my voice. I feel a sense of duty to do the same for those who come after me," added Pearl. It is this sense of duty and purpose that compels many of the participants to remain in academia and seek leadership positions. Pearl's statement exemplifies the sentiment.

> You do this for something bigger than yourself. We must understand our impact as women of color in academia; we have the capacity to make a positive difference and can make indelible contributions to nursing education and healthcare. So, we persevere. [Pearl]

Many participants expressed that diversity and inclusion in nursing academia are performative. Their persistence and contribution to the nursing academic world will advance diversity and inclusion in nursing.

#### Ending the diversity and inclusion myth

Nursing is a long way from achieving diversity and inclusion. The role and persistence of current Black nurse academics are critical for ending the current myth of diversity and inclusion. Although the participants all agreed that things have changed over the past five decades, they all believe that more needs to be done by all nurse academics. They expressed that finding a way to retain the nurses of color currently in nursing academia will attract many more because they will "not be the one and only."

Opal explained that as faculty and leaders, "we must strive to create an environment in our institutions where individuals of all cultures feel safe and confident to express themselves without fear of retaliation. Where students of color are treated as decently as White students."

#### Summary of Findings

The narratives described in this study highlight the experiences of Black nursing faculty and the need to provide a sense of belonging and eliminate a culture of silence in academia. Participants identified the adoption of resilience and the need to remain on duty to fight the good fight to be successful in their academic roles at their respective institutions. However, these experiences are not ideal for retaining and recruiting Black nurse faculty, which is needed to create a pipeline of Black faculty and nursing students.

#### Discussion

This current study illustrates stories of Black nursing faculty in academic institutions. Further, these stories reveal an unwavering spirit even when others did not embrace [our] strength and perceived us as not enough. This was also revealed in a study by Williams et al. (2020), who reported that academia, specifically White Institutions, often see [our] strengths as liabilities rather than signs of equality. Additionally, in this current study, participants shared that they felt forced to protect their very own existence of being Black. These findings are consistent with prior studies whereby Black faculty also discussed the need to protect their existence by distraction efforts to cope such as working independently, increasing their productivity (overworking), and reliance on spirituality and faith (Beard & Julian, 2016; Grandison et al., 2022; Puplampu et al., 2023; Louis et al., 2016; Mickles-Burns, 2023). As in the current study, faculty in prior research also reported the need to protect themselves by making extraordinary efforts to establish their credibility including working independently and being hypervigilance and extra prepared (Beard & Julion, 2016; Louis et al., 2016; Mickles-Burns, 2023; Puplampu et al., 2023). Participants in this study further explained these aforementioned efforts as new sets of skills needed to succeed, describing them as "nuanced understanding of applying the principles of emotional intelligence, meta-perception

positioning, cultural humility, and political prowess; these are the components needed to be Black faculty members."

The narratives in the stories in this study revealed that participants were made to feel a sense of guilt for being Black and for refusing to accept the stereotypical roles forced upon them. Some of these stereotypical roles in this study were expressed as feeling undervalued, especially when the question often posed to them by White counterparts was, how did you get here? Additionally, almost all the participants described being subjected to language (verbal and body) that projected a feeling that we do not count, which sometimes led them to feel less than others and doubt their abilities as faculty members. Further, it was evident in the stories that one must strive to prove worthiness to define yourself and what you are capable of in your position. Similar sentiments were expressed in prior studies whereby faculty felt the need to prove their legitimacy (Grandison et al., 2023; Mickles-Burns, 2023). This is akin to the concept mentioned in the book Becoming, authored by Michelle Obama, in which she said that "if you do not get out there and define yourself, you will be quickly and inaccurately defined by others" (p. 285).

In this study, spiritual faith was an everpresent undertone in a majority of our stories. In prior research, faculty also report reliance on faith and spirituality as coping mechanisms necessary for succeeding as a Black faculty member (Beard & Julian, 2016; Mickles-Burns, 2023; Puplampu et al., 2023). This outcome is unsurprising given the religious commitment among most Black people. Further, in this study, it is apparent that trust came from the performer (God), who they thought of as the processor to deliver the promise.

These stories conveyed the good, the bad, and the ugly as participants suffered in silence, were made to feel like outsiders, and endured ongoing microaggressions. In the current study, some of the participants discussed losing support from peers and superiors as they advanced in nursing academia. Thomas et al. (2013), described this type of loss of support as the pet-to-threat phenomenon. A phenomenon detrimental to the career advancement of Black women, whereby support is initially provided and then withdrawn when Black women progress in their careers. In prior research faculty also reported inadequate and loss of support (Beard

& Julian, 2016; Mickles-Burns, 2023) leading to feelings of isolation and being silenced (Pumplampu et al., 2023 Beard & Julian, 2016; Mickles-Burns, 2023). Furthermore, due to Black women's intersectional identity, they are viewed as the token hired to fulfill the organization's requirement for equal employment opportunity and diversity initiatives (Mickles-Burns, 2023; Thomas et al., 2013). In this current study, Black faculty conveyed that when they demonstrated professional competence and expressed aspirations for career advancement, they were viewed as a threat by superiors and colleagues, particularly if they did not conform to gender and racial stereotypes. These expressions are consistent with prior research studies where Black faculty at Predominantly White Institutions (PWI) and HBCUs report unsupportive work environments involving tokenism (Mickles-Burns, 2023) and withdrawal of support (Puplampu et al., 2023; Louis et al., 2016). This current study also revealed how, although these indignities injured [us] and challenged our strength, they also provoke a sense of duty. For these participants, strength and resilience transcended the confines of intersecting oppressions and fueled the need to create our own village. In prior studies, themes by faculty also revealed the need to be resilient (Beard & Julion, 2016; Puplampu et al., 2023). This is an ongoing attitude as evidenced by a few Black nurse trailblazers, Harriet Tubman, Sallie Allen Tucker, Goldie Brangman-Dumpson, and Estelle Massey Riddle Osborne, who had to endure the indignities and oppression that left cuts and scars to try to advance. For the Black nursing faculty in this current study, the village's foundation is laid through the mentoring received of being ABNF members and the legacy of the past and current trailblazers who have bestowed and empowered us to be more than enough.

#### Implications for Nursing Education

Diversity, equity, and inclusion are the way forward to global health. This concept came through with the 2019 pandemic spotlighting the role of Black nurse faculty in nursing education and practice. Despite the challenges, Black nurse faculty bring diverse skills and experiences as leaders and instructors working with students. Therefore, there is a need to focus on recruiting and retaining Black nurse faculty to align with the number of Black nursing students in different nursing programs and higher education. This article provides insight into

the historical relevance of Black nursing faculty and institutions promoting DEIB in nursing education. Promoting the recruitment and retention of Black nurse faculty and proffering strategies for a safe work environment for teaching and learning for the few Black nurse faculty in higher education is critical (Montgomery et al., 2021). This work also supports an evidence-based strategy to address the nursing faculty shortage, which addresses the global nursing shortage that critically impacts patient outcomes.

#### Implications for future research

The implications for research are similar to the qualitative findings of Iheduru-Anderson et al. (2022), which highlighted the need to provide mentoring, resources, and networking opportunities for Black nurse leaders in academia. Furthermore, Black nursing faculty have unique needs as they adapt to academic and scientific roles (Johnson et al., 2023). The success of Black nursing faculty is profoundly influenced by the mentoring received. In light of the need to address the underrepresentation of males in nursing academia, the perspectives of Black male nursing faculty to gain insight into their lived experience is necessary. Continuing exploration of the narratives of Black nurses with tenure and promotion can shed light on the strategies employed for success. The personal accounts of Black nurse faculty using quantitative and qualitative approaches could provide further insight. Additionally, these accounts should include Black nurses holding leadership positions.

#### Limitations

There are many strengths to storytelling through personal experience; however, the authors acknowledge the limitations of drawing upon personal memory data. The recall of experience may be distorted by the emotions evoked. Therefore, we do not treat our memory as an absolute representation of objective reality; we view our memory and recall as a reflection of what holds meaning for us as nursing faculty. Therefore, we confidently rely on these memories to reveal essential aspects of the experience (Cooper & Lilyea, 2022). Given the paucity of qualitative studies with this focus, this study provides a lens of perspectives that will serve to promote further study in larger populations. To ensure a comprehensive understanding, future research should aim for a gender-balanced sample

that includes nurse educators from diverse racial backgrounds, beyond just Black individuals as was the case in this study. This would allow for an examination of whether the findings hold consistent or diverge under varied demographic representations.

#### Conclusion

The experiences of these Black nursing faculty embody narratives of resilience, unwavering commitment to education, and transformative influence of their presence and roles. Study findings paint a comprehensive picture of diverse but related experiences, challenges, and successes that shape the professional journeys of these Black nursing educators. The hope is that these stories will transform and transcend other Black nursing educators to tell their stories and find meaning in what resilience looks like as a Black nurse educator. Leaders, policymakers, and social scientists have grappled for decades with how best to mitigate healthcare disparities that thwart progress in the U.S. healthcare system. A nursing workforce that reflects the diversity of the people served is thought to help bridge the divide. With a looming nursing shortage projected for the next decade, academic nursing is tapped to help solve the root of the problem. If the diversity of nurses is to reflect the population served, then it is imperative to critically examine upstream the experiences of ethnic or racial subgroups in academia, such as addressed in this study. Comprehensive findings of Black nursing academicians and leaders' lived experiences reveal a spectrum of diversity, equity, inclusivity, and belonging factors that must be strategically managed. Sense of duty, mentoring, and breaking the [negative] cycle are impactful for dismantling the culture of oppression and can transform the nursing profession towards a brighter future for improving healthcare.

## Experiences and challenges for African American women with hypertension during the COVID-19 pandemic

The persistence of hypertension remains a notable concern among ethnic minority groups, particularly African Americans, who exhibit a higher prevalence of hypertension at 41.8% and lower rates of blood pressure control at 42.7%, in contrast to Whites with a prevalence of 34.2% and control rates of 51.1% (Aggarwal et al., 2021). Notably, African American women suffer from a significantly higher prevalence of hypertension at 55.3% compared to Caucasian women at 40.5%, Hispanic women at 40.8%, and Asian women at 42.1% (Virani et al., 2021). Various factors contribute to the differences in prevalence of hypertension found among African American and White women. These factors include socioeconomic issues such as poverty, limited access to nutritious food, inadequate healthcare access, and insufficient knowledge about hypertension management (Ferdinand et al., 2020). It has also been determined that hypertension is associated with a higher risk of heart failure, an increased risk for cardiovascular disease (CVD) when left untreated, and a 67% higher risk of recurrent stroke (Tsao et al., 2023). These statistics underscore the disproportionate impact of hypertension on African American women.

Research suggests that increased awareness leads to positive dietary changes that can effectively lower blood pressure among African Americans with hypertension (Wright et al., 2021; Blumenthal et al., 2021). Despite promising interventions that effectively managed hypertension in this population, progress was hindered by the COVID-19 pandemic. The pandemic significantly increased the risk of morbidity and mortality among African Americans with hypertension, exacerbating the burden of hypertension within this population (Ferdinand et al., 2020).

During the COVID-19 pandemic, adherence to a healthy diet and physical activity decreased. African American, Hispanic, and other minority groups were more likely to experience food insecurity during the COVID-19 pandemic than White or Asian groups (Morita & Kato, 2023). Individuals experiencing food insecurity face a risk for hypertension that is 2.4 times greater than those who have not experienced food insecurity (Gundersen & Ziliak, 2015).

Importantly, African American women who were able to access sufficient food during the pandemic may have been more likely to engage in unhealthy eating habits. Many African American women reported challenges with eating behavior during this time (Goode et al., 2022). Challenges women experienced included limited food availability and accessibility. Many of the participants purchased food that they would not regularly eat because it was inexpensive and nonperishable. These food items are often processed foods that are high in fat, salt, and sugar (Goode et al., 2022). Women indicated that they needed to purchase these food items to maintain an adequate food supply while being homebound for several weeks. These limitations in food availability and accessibility limited healthy food options for participants, resulting in challenges in maintaining healthy eating behaviors (Goode et al., 2022).

Nienhuis and Lesser (2020) found that women reported more barriers to physical activity than men did during the pandemic and thus were less physically active. These barriers included lack of enjoyment, self-consciousness, and time constraints, as well as increased domestic and child-care responsibilities (Nienhuis & Lesser, 2020). Furthermore, diet quality among African Americans decreased as affordability, lack of access to food, and perception of the pandemic, were barriers to a healthier diet during the COVID-19 pandemic (Kibe et al., 2023). As African Americans faced higher rates of unemployment and lower wages, as well as barriers to internet access and COVID-19 information, their ability and desire to maintain a healthy diet were impaired (Kibe et al., 2023). This was accompanied by an increase in psychosocial stress. A study by Kalinowski et al. (2022) found that the intersectional stressors imposed on African American women were exacerbated during the COVID-19 pandemic. These stressors included an increased likelihood of contracting the virus, financial instability, burdens due to working from home, caregiving, and coinciding realities of racial injustice and COVID-19, which was described as a "twin pandemic" (Kalinowski et al., 2022).

A major contributing factor to the management of hypertension during the pandemic was reduced or limited access to routine health care. Individuals reported challenges such as a lack of health insurance coverage or affordability, minimal guidance navigating the health care system, and physical proximity to health care services (Ingram et al., 2022). Additionally, older African American adults reported that their telehealth experiences were impersonal and difficult to access due to barriers to internet access and knowledge (Ingram et al., 2022).

Unemployment and underemployment were also shown to prevent African American women from properly self-managing their hypertension. In one study, nearly half (48%) of African American women reported that the top two sources of their stress were from household bills and other expenses (Javaid & Cole, n.d.). Further evidence provided by the National Women's Law Center reported that African American women were less likely to have salaried jobs and more likely to have hourly jobs making \$15/hour or less compared to White men (Javaid & Cole, n.d.).

Importantly, there was a lack of community resources providing proper information about COVID-19 and ways to access health care during the pandemic. Chandler et al. (2021) found that 79% of African American women felt confused and a sense of mistrust about the information they were accessing regarding COVID-19, and 46% indicated that the pandemic impacted their or their child's health care (Chandler et al., 2021). In addition to these challenges, there was the overarching burden of

social distance policies. For example, social connectedness was disrupted; with the inability to join together for important family functions, individuals felt increasing disempowerment, helplessness, and disconnectedness (Kalinowski et al., 2022).

African American women faced unique challenges in the management of hypertension during the COVID-19 pandemic. Environmental, social, and economic factors exacerbated existing challenges in managing hypertension. Existing studies have applied quantitative approaches to examine the burden of the pandemic on the management of hypertension, but few have used qualitative approaches to explore the experiences of African American women living with hypertension during the pandemic and the barriers they faced in the management of hypertension. The objective of this study is to examine the experiences and challenges African American women faced while managing hypertension during the pandemic.

#### Methods

#### Research Design

This study employed a qualitative approach through focus group methodology to understand the experiences and challenges African American women experienced while attempting to self-manage their hypertension during COVID-19. The aim of utilizing this design was to delve into, comprehend, and unearth insights regarding a specific phenomenon via group dynamics and dialogue (Creswell & Creswell, 2018). This approach resonated with the research objectives and methods. Despite quantitative metrics available for hypertension self-management, the study sought to glean firsthand experiences from participants in managing their reported hypertension amidst the pandemic's peak. Given the imperative to grasp the lived realities within this demographic, a qualitative methodology was deemed more fitting for this investigation (Denzin & Lincoln, 2003; Liamputtong, 2011; Creswell & Creswell, 2018).

#### Sample/Setting

Purposive sampling sample consisted of 30 African American women who self-reported a diagnosis of hypertension aged 18 years and older living in the Northeast region of the United States.

Participants were recruited predominantly from local food pantries, churches, homeless shelters, and community health centers. Participants who were pregnant or who had severe speech impairments, profound visual impairments or profound deafness were excluded from the study. All interviews were conducted via Zoom due to the need for social distancing and followed Health Insurance Portability and Accountability (HIPAA) guidelines.

#### **Procedures**

Because of the peak of the COVID-19 pandemic, we refrained from requesting participants to visit for an objective blood pressure measurement or to ascertain their hypertension status. Therefore, participants voluntarily opted into the study based on their own acknowledgment of being diagnosed with hypertension. Data collection relied on participants' self-reported hypertension status. Given the imperative for social distancing, hypertension assessment was not conducted during the focus group sessions, as it did not align with the study's objectives. The study aimed to comprehend the experiences of African American women based on their self-reported hypertension diagnoses.

A total of six 60-to-90 minute focus group sessions consisting of four to eight individuals in each group were utilized as the primary source of data collection in the study from January 2021- March 2021. Participants were identified in the tape recordings and were given the option of using a pseudonym. To protect confidentiality, identifying information was deleted from the transcript for participants who did not use a pseudonym.

#### Instruments

A 10-item demographic questionnaire was completed by the participants prior to the focus group session. Additionally, a 12-item semi-structured interview guide was used to guide the focus group discussions. The researcher developed the demographic questionnaire and interview guide, which underwent validation and approval by two content experts. Multiple iterations were undertaken before their implementation. reliability and validity. Following the approval of the interview guide and demographic questionnaire by the content experts, the researcher tested the questions with a purposive sample of two participants. This process contributed to enhancing the reliability and validity of the tools.

#### Data Analysis

Audio recordings of the focus group sessions were transcribed verbatim to text by a professional transcription service for analysis. Once data were transcribed, the coders read the transcripts to gain familiarity with the overall meaning, tone, and depth of the information (Creswell & Creswell, 2018). Data were analyzed using content analysis. Through an iterative coding process, three coders read each transcript, highlighting keywords and phrases. After open coding two transcripts, the researchers decided on preliminary codes. After all the transcripts were coded, the coders confirmed alignment and assigned labels to codes that emerged. Codes were then sorted into categories and subcategories. Definitions of each category and subcategory were developed (Hsieh & Shannon, 2005). Then, the researchers assigned final names to themes and subthemes and wrote up a description of the themes and subthemes.

#### Ethical Considerations

The study proceeded following approval from the Ursuline College Institutional Review Board, IRB # 20-29, whose task is to ensure that research participants are protected from harm. The researcher maintained neither influence nor authority over participants. Upon completion of the focus group sessions, each participant received a \$30 grocery store or Walmart gift card.

#### Discussion

African-American women managing hypertension during the COVID-19 pandemic expressed financial, social, and environmental barriers that impacted their ability to self-manage hypertension. Participants reported that they experienced stress and anxiety due to social isolation and being unable to connect with their family members due to social distancing recommendations. During the pandemic, serving as a caregiver to older adults, friends, and children became more common, and the participants in the study shared their challenges living in multigenerational households with parents, children, and grandparents all residing in the same household. Participants also expressed challenges trying to work and maintain employment while providing care for older adults and the challenges of having children at home engaging in school online.

The pandemic had direct effects on the cost of food and individuals' ability to afford healthy foods and challenges locating healthy foods and resources that were available prior to the pandemic. Access to health care was a key challenge cited by participants, first the impact of limited transportation options to get to healthy foods or medical appointments but also the immediate transition from in-person appointments to virtual appointments for medical care, which proved to be a challenge for many individuals managing health conditions. Last, participants shared the importance of managing high blood pressure, specifically concerns about increasing blood pressure, changes in diet because of being home, such as increased salt intake, and their efforts to manage high blood pressure by modifying their diet but also increasing their physical activity at home.

Our findings are similar to Adesogan et al. (2022), in which African American women's physical health worsened and depression symptoms increased after the onset of the COVID-19 pandemic. Women also experienced sleep problems after the onset of the COVID-19 pandemic; however, participants in the current study did not report any sleep pattern disturbances. Additionally, worsening blood pressure for individuals with hypertension that was associated with the COVID-19 pandemic is

similar to the findings in the current study. The lack of blood pressure control was attributed to limited access to telemedicine, loss of health insurance due to unemployment, and cancellations or postponements of in-person health care appointments to avoid potential exposure.

Although this study was not exclusive to African American women, these results are similar to the current study findings, which indicated that the COVID-19 pandemic created barriers for individuals with chronic health conditions such as hypertension, especially for African American women, to effectively manage their hypertension (Gotanda et al., 2022). African American women also experienced high rates of food insecurity, which is similar to our study findings (Coats et al., 2022). African American women also experienced stress related to caring for children who were homebound and needed to be homeschooled, as well as providing care for family and friends. These findings are similar to those found in the current study (Kalinowski et al., 2022).

#### Strengths/Limitations

This study has both strengths and limitations worth noting. The study did not include data on other comorbid conditions, such as diabetes or high cholesterol, which may have impacted how an individual manages both their diet and physical activity level. Additionally, the study participants were all African American women with hypertension, and their experiences may not be representative of the broader population or reflective of the collective experiences of African American women in the United States.

Moreover, hypertension was self-reported by participants rather than being objectively measured, which may be subject to recall bias. Finally, we lack information regarding the participants' circumstances before the pandemic. It is important to note that, for this study, we could not ascertain the individual situations of participants before the pandemic. Nonetheless, the pandemic brought about challenges for many individuals, resulting in overall more difficult circumstances compared to pre-pandemic times.

A strength of this study is the utilization of qualitative research methods, which allows for a deeper understanding of the experiences of the research participants in their journey with

hypertension self-management during COVID-19. We collected qualitative data from women about their experiences managing hypertension during a global pandemic, whereas many of the existing studies have applied quantitative measures to assess experiences during the COVID-19 pandemic. Last, to assure reproducibility, dependability, and precision, the qualitative research process was well defined using a single-category focus group.

#### Public Health Implications

This study informs the field of public health by identifying specific hypertension-related barriers exacerbated by the COVID-19 pandemic that disproportionately harm African American women. By encouraging participants to share their challenges with hypertension, public health officials can tailor health interventions that are not only culturally relevant but also targeted to address the concerns expressed by these participants in the context of pandemic recovery. For instance, interventions can increase the availability and delivery of community resources, virtual accessibility to contact health care providers, and address social and cultural isolation. These results also speak to the resilience of African American women as a community, specifically in regard to managing hypertension in adverse conditions, which provides an area for further public health research.

#### Nursing Implications

Understanding the unique experiences and challenges that African American women with hypertension encountered during COVID-19 is critical for health care professionals to provide effective health care and support. These experiences and challenges may continue to perpetuate health outcomes for African American women long after the COVID-19 pandemic. Therefore, nurses and other health care providers must address the social determinants of health identified in this study and include these issues as part of the plan of care. In addition, African American women also experience both emotional and psychological effects of the COVID-19 pandemic, which may have affected their ability to manage their hypertension; therefore, offering community resources such as stress management support and counseling can help African American women overcome these barriers (Kreutz et al., 2021).

In addition, nurses can work with other health care professionals as well as community organizations and advocacy groups that will promote equity access to health care services to address health disparities (Ferdinand et al., 2020). Finally, nurses and other health care providers can schedule regular follow-up appointments to assess and address any challenges that African American women may experience when managing their hypertension so that adjustments can be made to their plan of care.

#### Conclusion

These study findings exemplified the heightened level of physical and mental stressors that women experienced during the COVID-19 pandemic, which exacerbated the current health disparities that exist in this population. Future studies should explore the long-term impact of poor blood pressure control related to the COVID-19 pandemic on the population. Nurses and other health care providers should begin their efforts to address the lack of access to health care that occurred during the COVID-19 pandemic to mitigate future emergencies. Finally, future research should develop effective interventions that will address the identified barriers to self-management of hypertension for African American women.

## Teaching Evidence-Based Practice to MSN, DNP and PhD students: Lessons Learned

#### Abstract

Background: An evidence-based practice (EBP) course containing Master of Nursing (MSN), Doctor of Nursing Practice (DNP) and Doctor of Philosophy (PhD) nursing students (N=76) was offered during a 12-week Spring/Summer semester. The intent of this evaluation is to assess the effectiveness and feasibility of a combined graduate level EBP course.

Method: A pre-posttest design was used. Anonymous, de-identified demographic questions and the Student Evidence Based Practice Questionnaire (SEBPQ) were used for data collection.

Results: The results indicated a significant difference pre vs post course in the areas of EBP use (Sig <0.001; F 15.806), EBP Skill (Sig <0.001, F 43.250) and EBP Communication (Sig <0.001; F 27.566). However, 22 out of the 24 students that reported feeling overwhelmed by the course were MSN students (91.7%).

Conclusion: The facilitation, evaluation, and redesign of EBP curriculum need to be informed by data gathered from formal assessments.

Key Words: MSN and DNP collaborative education, Evidence-based practice

#### Introduction

There are multiple challenges in advanced nursing education today. One significant challenge is meeting the needs of educational demands for evidence-based practice and faculty shortages. The faculty shortage combined with the growth of enrollment in DNP program (~4%) and MSN program (3.8%) nationally and slight decrease in PhD program (0.7%) created an opportunity to be creative in an innovative approach to teaching evidence-based practice (Bakewell-Sachs et al.,

2022). This paper describes the development of a multidegree approach to an evidence-based practice course that combines Master of Science, Doctor of Nursing Practice, and Philosophy Doctorate degree students in one evidence-based nursing course.

Nearly 20 years ago, the American Association College of Nursing (AACN) declared that the Doctor of Nursing Practice (DNP) degree was the entry into practice for advanced practice registered nurses (APRNs). Once this declaration was made, it was expected that institutions of higher education that prepared APRNs would transition to the DNP program by 2015 (AACN, 2004). Despite the ambitious goals of AACN, in the year 2024, most universities have not completely transitioned Master of Science in Nursing (MSN) programs and currently offer both MSN and DNP programs (Auerbach et al., 2015).

 Colleges of nursing that continue to maintain both MSN and DNP programs are constantly considering innovative approaches to provide quality learning experiences while balancing faculty shortages and increased student enrollment. In an effort to increase collaboration among graduate nursing students and maximize faculty utilization, a decision was made to offer a graduate level evidencebased course which was inclusive of Master of Nursing (MSN) students, Doctor of Nursing Practice (DNP) students and Doctor of Philosophy (PhD) nursing students. In preparation for this new course structure, the faculty of record reviewed the literature regarding MSN, DNP and PhD joint evidence-based courses and the pedagogical approaches for teaching evidence-based practice to a combined cohort of graduate nursing students (MSN, DNP, PhD).

Evidence based practice is defined as a deliberate and conscientious approach to problemsolving within clinical practice. An evidence-based approach embodies the best evidence from welldesigned studies while taking into consideration patient values, preferences, and a clinician's expertise in making decisions regarding patient's care (Melnyk and Fineout-Overholt, 2019). Discernment related to evidence-based practice and an understanding of the levels applied to evidence are important skills for clinicians to have. These skills allow the clinician to be confident in their prioritization of studies, reports and practice guidelines when making decisions about a patient's care (AACN, 2023). There is no argument that the understanding and utilization of EBP is an important skill cultivated within clinical and research-based education, however, the extent to which EBP is actualized and implemented does vary within formal nursing training programs and is distinctly different for MSN and DNP students.

Reviewing the literature for evidence-based nursing courses that include MSN, DNP and PhD students concurrently did not produce any peerreviewed articles to support combining categories of graduate nursing students nor did any manuscript provide insight into the most effective pedagogical approach for teaching evidence-based practice to MSN, DNP and PhD students concurrently in the same class section.

An article published by Moore et al. (2019) evaluated MSN and DNP students separately in their respective graduate level evidence-based courses. The study conducted by Moore et al., (2019) highlighted similarities and differences between the MSN and DNP students regarding evidence-based practice (EBP) knowledge and utilization pre and post enrollment in a graduate level EBP course. The study was conducted using a two group, pre and posttest design evaluating 102 MSN students and 24 DNP students. The findings from this study indicated that DNP students entered into EBP courses with more experience with evidence-based implementation in clinical settings. Furthermore, it was found that DNP students had more self-rated confidence in their abilities to implement EBP and had a statistically significant difference from pre and post EBP courses when knowledge scores were assessed. Positive change in EBP behavior pre and post course was statistically significant among MSN students. Overall, student scores significantly increased for both MSN and DNP students in the domains of EBP attitude and self-efficacy from pre to post course for both groups of students. This study

highlights that there are inherent differences between MSN and DNP students entering a graduate level EBP course. Moore et al. (2019) encourages faculty to be cognizant of the unique learning needs of MSN and DNP students and use pre/posttest course evaluation to assess student learning.

#### A Combined EBP Course

In 2022 the College of Nursing at an urban university in the Midwest decided to change the structure of the graduate level EBP course which included combining MSN, DNP and PhD into one section. Furthermore, this change resulted in the largest enrollment seen on the EBP course with 76 graduate students registered. Of note, traditionally, this particular EBP course was exclusively a DNP seminar which averaged twenty-five students per section. This EBP class was offered over a 12-week Spring/Summer semester (May-July) and meet for 3 hours every week. This EBP course is a required foundational course for DNP students and builds on other foundational courses that they need to implement their DNP project which is a graduation requirement at this College of Nursing. With the new change in curriculum, this EBP course (which was originally designed for DNP students only) is now considered a core course for MSN and PhD students, however, implementing an EBP project is not a graduation requirement for MSN or PhD students at this university. The MSN, DNP, and PhD curriculum committees approved the addition of the EBP course to the curriculum to address deficits in EBP education across the graduate curriculum and to enhance interprofessional collaboration.

#### Literature Review

In examining the evidence for making changes an appraisal of current knowledge was completed. A systematic review of electronic bibliographic databases (CINAHL, Medline, PsychInfo, and Pub Med) utilizing the search terms evidence-based practice (EBP), teaching EBP, faculty shortage, nursing enrollment and teaching innovation, collaborative coursework for the period January 2004 to March 2024 was completed. The search was supplemented by a manual review of reference lists for each article. Finally, the American Association of Colleges of Nursing website was reviewed for further information. Articles were selected if a relationship between the terms was obvious.

#### Approaches to Evidence-Based Practice

Melnyk et al. (2012) describes evidencebased practice (EBP) as a clinical decision-making tool that improves clinical outcomes. EBP has a proven track record of improving quality of care and patient outcomes (McGinty and Anderson, 2008). Evidence-based practice as a problem-solving approach fits with Institute of Medicine (IOM) recommendations that all health professionals translate the research into practice (Institute of Medicine, 2003). What is important is educating graduate students to implement EBP by improving their knowledge and skills on critical appraisal of evidence, assess needs, and strategies to implement EBP.

One of the issues with the translation of evidence into practice is ensuring that the graduate nursing students have competencies in EBP. According to Hande et al. (2017), MSN students enter the program with the knowledge to evaluate the usefulness of nursing research that is built on their baccalaureate nursing. The education of EBP for the MSN graduates should prepare them to critically appraise and translate evidence to improve patient outcomes (Hande et al., 2017). Doctor of Nursing Practice (DNP) programs prepare graduates to improve patient outcomes and translate research into practice (AACN, 2023b). The DNP should be prepared to address the increasing complexity of healthcare delivery and focus on improved quality, cost, and access through the translation of evidence into practice. EBP for the DNP prepared students requires evidence synthesis, translation of evidence into practice as demonstrated by the implementation of a scholarly project. The Doctor of Philosophy (Ph.D.) in nursing is about addressing the gaps in the literature thus the PhD students need to be able to critically appraise research, identify the gaps and propose alternative hypothesis to determine the next level of evidence (Burkem et. al., 2005). PhD students need competency in EBP since they will be the generators of new knowledge. Most importantly, these PhD prepared graduates must be able to generate new knowledge or evidence that can be translated into evidence expeditiously by their DNP colleagues.

#### Faculty Shortage and Enrollment Demand

 American Association of Colleges of Nursing (AACN) (2023) has been tracking the nursing faculty shortage for years and in the most recent survey there was a full-time vacancy rate in the Midwest of ~6.9%. Finding new faculty is hampered by discrepancy between clinical job salaries and faculty salaries. Additional barriers for faculty recruitment and retention include the need for faculty to possess the appropriate advanced practice registered nursing specialty needed at the college, the dearth of doctorally prepared faculty and the aging and imminent retirement of many faculty (AACN, 2022). Fang and Keston (2017) reported that the percentage of faculty over 6o years old was 30.7% in 2015, and that number is only going to continue to rise (Jaronski et al., 2022). Issues of lack of funds to hire new faculty, administrative barriers, increased competition for faculty, and geographic differences in shortages also contribute to the faculty shortage (Jaronski et al., 2022). The disparities related to faculty shortages directly impact student admissions and it was estimated that over 91,938 potential qualified nursing students were turned away from baccalaureate and graduate nursing programs (AACN, 2023).

#### Innovations in Teaching Joint Degree Courses

Advanced Practice Registered Nurses (APRNs) are registered nurses (RN) with advanced degrees (MSN, DNP, PhD) that make important contributions to the improvement in the quality of healthcare provided and the advancement of nursing science. While each of the degrees are different, they have complementary roles and make exploration of the use of EBP within the discipline of nursing exciting (Troutman et al., 2018). For APRNs that want to meet the increasing demands of quality care it is imperative that they can apply credible research findings, translate the evidence into practice, and work well with PhD prepared nurse scientists and clinicians that are addressing the research gaps that impact patients, nursing, and healthcare today. If the gap between translation of research into practice is to be addressed, it is key that these graduate students are able to synthesize the evidence with critical appraisal skills that ensure practitioners are using the most accurate approaches (Moore et al., 2019).

Innovations in teaching evidence-based courses have benefited from early work of those that promoted the use of EBP. In nursing we have done

an excellent job of teaching nurses about research and the importance of research but not such an excellent job in how to use this research (Krainovich-Miller et al., 2007). To maximize the cooperation in these complimentary degree roles it is important to set strategic goals when teaching combined MSN, DNP, and PhD students. Providing joint course content as an approach maximizes efficiencies in faculty workload through a model of program delivery (Edward et.al., 2016). In addition, teaching the MSN, DNP, and PhD students in the same EBP course promotes collaboration and can emphasize how the various degrees can work together. Joint courses are a strategy that promotes collaboration and collegiality (Edward et al., 2016). In their work Edward et al., (2016) concluded that joint courses maximized resource efficiencies, improved collaboration, and collegiality, and contributed to student success.

Unfortunately, there is a dearth of evidence about teaching joint courses across degrees. There is research about interprofessional education, importance of teaching evidence-based approaches across all levels of curriculum, and the wellestablished benefits of EBP (Melnyk et al., 2009). There has been work on enhancing learning in larger classrooms that encourages student engagement and depth of understanding of the evidence-based practice principles (Veter and Latimer, 2017). Veter and Latimer (2017) discussed the imperatives of generation learning changes to multimodal digital approaches, working groups and the flipped classroom approach. In their work, most students identified the approach of flipped classrooms that helped them learn the concepts (Veter and Latimer, 2017). They discussed the successes of students that were engaged in learning, however there were also challenges identified that proved to be barriers to knowledge acquisition (Veter and Latimer, 2017).

What is evident is leveraging innovative teaching models that maximize the utilization of faculty and improve student learning are needed. There is a need for teaching models that address larger classroom sizes, teaching efficiency, and interaction of graduate students from differing degree programs.

#### Methods

#### Multimodal Pedagogy

Multimodal instruction in graduate nursing programs is an effective approach for enhancing graduate program curriculum (Elertson and Lehr, 2021). In planning for the best evidence-based approaches to teaching EBP to a mixed cohort of MSN, DNP and PhD, the faculty elected to use a multimodal pedagogical approach.

Seventy-six (76) graduate students were divided into evidence-based practice teams (3-4 students per team), each team had at least 1 DNP and 1 MSN student (24 Teams in total). Each team was assigned a specific health disparity and population (i.e., colorectal screening in communities of color). The main assignment of the course was a "mock" evidence-based implementation project that was developed by the evidence-based teams as they went through all of the steps of evidence-based practice. The EBP teams were required to identify and justify an evidenced-based intervention to address the health disparity. The campus librarian assigned to the College of Nursing was invited to the class and spent over 2 hours reviewing how to access articles, use search engines within the library system, narrow down evidence-based searches and gather resources required for the course. The main textbook for the course was Evidence-based Practice in Nursing & Healthcare: A Guide to Best Practice (4th edition) (Melnyk & Fineout-Overholt, 2019). The flipped classroom portion of the course followed the chapters in this textbook and the chapters were assigned weekly as required readings. The EBP teams were assigned chapters in the textbook as well as journal articles to summarize for the class, followed by learning activities such as quizzes and group discussion questions that reinforced learning. Guest speakers that had completed quality improvement projects in the clinical setting were invited to present their project, EBP process and outcomes to the class to offer a real-life example of EBP in practice. There was a total of four completed quality improvement projects presented to the class throughout the semester.

The major assignment for the course was an evidence-based implementation project proposal (worth 75% of the total grade). This proposal was the continuous work of the evidence-based practice groups throughout the semester with pieces of the proposal due at different weeks to obtain feedback. The main components of the proposal were an Introduction, Goals and Aims of the project, SMART

objectives (at least 3), Significance to Nursing, State of the Science, Theoretical Framework, Methodological Framework and Project Implementation (Setting, Organizational Culture, Participants, Indicator Measures, Procedures, Evaluation Plan and Dissemination). At Week 5, the Introductory paragraph, State of the Science, detailed manuscript outline, evidence-based intervention Comparison table, and ten (10) references were due to the faculty. Faculty feedback was given to the groups and the group made revisions as needed. Week 7, the project proposals were submitted for anonymous peer review. Each group was assigned another group's de-identified proposal to review in terms of clarity and adherence to the rubric. Peer review feedback was provided back to the authors of the proposal via an anonymous Qualtrics survey. Final proposals were due Week 10 to faculty and the teams presented their ten-minute project PowerPoints highlighting their EBP process for their given topics Week 10 through Week 12.

#### Methods for evaluating the course

Student Evaluations of Teaching (SET) surveys administered by the university are very generic and have typically been the only method of evaluating teaching effectiveness for courses. However, these evaluations do not assess specific measures related to the students' self-efficacy and ability to use EBP (Moore et al., 2019). Students enrolled in this combined EBP course were asked to complete an anonymous Qualtrics survey at the beginning of the 12-week semester and at the end. Demographic information was collected in addition to data from the Student Evidence-Based Practice Questionnaire (SEBPQ) which is valid and reliable questionnaire measuring the effectiveness of EBP education and intent to utilize EBP (Upton et al., 2015; Mitra et al., 2019). Finally, the post course survey had open-ended questions and collected qualitative feedback from students regarding the course. This protocol was evaluated by Wayne State University IRB and deemed to be quality improvement which did not require IRB review (WSU IRB HPR Number 2022 145).

#### Effectiveness of EBP education

The tools used for data collection was an anonymous electronic Qualtrics survey, which included de-identified demographic questions and the Student Evidence-Based Practice Questionnaire (SEBPQ). The SEBPQ was designed by Upton et al. (2015) and was modified from the Evidence-Based Practice Questionnaire (EBPQ) created in 2006 (Upton and Upton, 2006). The SEBPQ is a standardized tool that measures evidence-based practice use (6 items), attitudes (3 items), knowledge and skills (regarding the retrieval and evaluation of evidence) (7 items), and the application and sharing of EBP (7 items) for nursing students using a Likert scale. The maximum score for each item was seven with a minimal score of one. The SEBPQ had a total maximum point of 147 with a minimum score of 21. SPSS was utilized for descriptive analysis of the data, including mean, standard deviation, percentage, frequency, and distribution table. Permission was granted via email for the utilization of the SEBPQ tool.

#### Results

#### Demographics

During a 12-week Spring/Summer semester in 2022 (May-July) a cohort of seventy-six (76) graduate nursing students (MSN, DNP, PhD) were enrolled in the graduate level evidence-based practice course, 66 females (86.8%) and 10 males (13.2%). MSN students accounted for 50% (38), DNP students accounted for 48.7%. (37) and there was one (1) PhD student which accounted for 1.3%. Most of the students were between the ages of 26 - 45 years of age (51, 67.1%). Seventy-one students (93.4%) entered the course with a bachelor's degree as their highest level of education. 84.2% of the graduate students had less than three semesters of graduate studies prior to enrolling into the evidence-based course. 32.9% (25) of the students had 6 to 10 years work experience as a registered nurse, 27% (21) had less than two years as a registered nurse and 18.4% (14) had 3 to 5 years registered nurse experience. 96.1% of students were currently working as a registered nurse.

#### Quantitative Measures: SEBPQ

The results from this concurrent cohort of MSN, DNP and PhD students showed a significant difference pre vs post course in the areas of EBP use (Sig <0.001; F 15.806), EBP Skill (Sig <0.001, F 43.250), EBP Communication (Sig <0.001; F 27.566). EBP Attitude did not show a significant

difference from pre to posttest (Sig. .422; F.649). Forty-one students (63.08%) felt that the course requirements were "appropriate," however 24 students (36.92%) stated that the course expectations were "overwhelming." 22 out of the 24 students that reported feeling overwhelmed by the course were MSN students (91.7%).

#### Qualitative Measures: Student Feedback

Direct quotes from the post survey: "{the course} felt more directed to DNP students. didn't seem to apply for MSN."

"The course was overwhelming for the MSN students, as they had never seen a lot of this before. This in turn was very overwhelming for me as it was my job to try to teach two individuals' basics for this class and try to formulate a project together." "I was among the handful of MSN students who entered this course as a final course of the program with no prior graduate level research course. This was overwhelming and thankfully my group members were DNP students and had already taken research courses to help me in contributing to the group paper. At first, I was disappointed that we were doing group work when first announced on the first day, but after the semester ended, I can see that flying solo as an MSN would have been unrealistic. Also, the general feeling was one of fear to bring up questions or express oneself and the struggles many students were having, especially the MSN students. #### Discussion

#### Barriers

The variation of prior knowledge between MSN and DNP students (i.e., SMART Goals, PICOT etc.) proved to be a barrier for many MSN students. MSN students expressed that they had not been exposed to this level of EBP information prior to enrolling in the graduate level EBP course. Furthermore, MSN students at this university are not

required to complete a final quality improvement project prior to graduation. In contrast, the DNP students at this university are required to complete a final DNP project that includes either implementing a quality improvement project, conducting a program evaluation or a policy analysis prior to graduation. Some MSN students (28.9%) only had one or two semesters of graduate school prior to taking this course while DNP students had three or more semesters. Most MSN students stated they needed more instruction or more time spent on foundational EBP concepts (i.e. PICOT question, SMART goals). Despite these issues, statistical analysis of pre/post surveys show significant improvement in numerous EBP domains at the end of the course compared to before enrolling in the course. Based on the qualitative data, MSN students valued group learning and found that being paired with a DNP student was integral to their success in the course. In contrast, some DNP students felt that the extent and exposure to evidence-based practice and core concepts were distinctly different for the MSN students and expressed concerns of having to mentor the MSN student throughout the course.

#### Conclusions: Lessons Learned

Collaboration among graduate students contributes to a rich learning environment for both the students and faculty. As we explore ways to enhance the learning experience and maximize faculty resources, we must also keep in mind the unique learning expectation ingrained within each graduate program and assess potential barriers to combining graduate level nursing courses. While some graduate courses may serve as a better medium for collaboration between MSN, DNP and PhD students, integral courses such as EBP would provide a better learning experience remaining separated especially if the program expectations are different for each program. Program specific EBP courses allow faculty to truly focus on the distinct learning outcomes that are unique to each graduate program and facilitate a student-centered learning environment that positions the student for greater success and satisfaction with the EBP experience.

It is also important for faculty to incorporate quality improvement and evidence-based practice in the facilitation, evaluation, and redesign of curriculum. Knowledge gained from quality improvement initiatives and evidence-based

assessments of teaching allow for informed decisions that are based on quantitative and qualitative data thus providing a better assessment of teaching effectiveness and will serve as a consistent guide for improvement and a measure of teaching success.

## Newest Vital Sign Health Literacy Assessment Tool Use in a Primary Care Clinic

#### **Abstract**

Background: Poor health literacy affects healthcare delivery and outcomes.

Activity: The purpose of this pilot study is to explore the use and feasibility of the Newest Vital Sign Health Literacy (NVS HL) assessment tool in a primary care clinic.

Implementation: This study utilized a mixedmethod approach, creating a bi-level data collection model assessing data from two sources: patients (n=30) and clinic nursing staff (n=7). The NVS HL assessment tool consisted of six questions and took less than five minutes to complete.

Results: Over half (56.7%) of patients' NVS scores indicated an adequate level of health literacy, 20% indicated a possibility of limited health literacy, and 23.3% indicated a high likelihood of limited health literacy. Study results indicated 71% of clinic nursing staff considered the NVS a reliable and feasible instrument for use in the clinic setting; 85.7% considered using the results from the NVS to modify treatment plans.

Keywords: newest vital sign, health literacy, primary care

The research discussed in the current manuscript has been IRB approved.

#### Introduction

The Agency for Healthcare Research and Quality (AHRQ) defines health literacy as the capacity to obtain, process and understand basic health information and services and make appropriate healthcare decisions or act on health information (AHRQ, 2017). Furthermore, Healthy People 2030 defines health literacy as when society provides accurate health information and services that people can easily find, understand, and use to inform their decisions and actions (U.S. Department of Health and Human Services, 2020).

Low health literacy is associated with having higher medical costs and inefficient use of healthcare services. For example, there is an increasing use of emergency services in comparison to primary care. An estimate of \$73 billion annually is the national cost associated with inadequate health literacy among adults. More than one third of Americans have limited health literacy (Housten et al., 2021). Individuals with lower health literacy have more instances of poor physical health, poor mental health, reduced health outcomes, and increased rates of obesity (Jayasinghe et al., 2016). These effects are associated with an increased number of hospital admissions, a greater number of ambulance transports, longer hospital stays, and more psychiatrist consults (Vandenbosch et al., 2016). Health literacy is a compelling public health issue that plays a significant role in the overall well-being of the domestic population and the national economy.

Low health literacy is especially prevalent among disadvantaged and vulnerable populations, and it contributes significantly toward the disproportionate burden of disease-related problems faced by these groups. Vulnerable populations include racial and ethnic minorities, the elderly, those seeking care in public healthcare facilities, and patients with chronic health conditions (Schillinger, 2021). Efforts in disease prevention, health promotion, and chronic disease self-management are less effective due to a lack of clear understanding of health literacy among individuals, populations, and healthcare services.

The most common health literacy screening instruments available, and often mentioned in the literature, include the Test of Functional Health Literacy in Adults (TOFHLA) and the

Rapid Estimate of Adult Literacy in Medicine (REALM) (Louis et al., 2017; AHRQ, 2019). Both instruments have good psychometric properties, but their limitations hinder their functionality in practice (Louis et al., 2017). TOFHLA, along with its shortened format (TOFHLA-SF), are available in both English and Spanish and are known to take too long to administer (TOFHLA takes 22 minutes, TOFHLA-SF takes 10 minutes) in a busy clinical setting (Sawyers et al., 2022).

Several studies have touted the Newest Vital Sign (NVS) as a quick and reliable screening tool for limited health literacy. In a study by Weiss et al. (2005), the NVS was administered to 500 participants, of whom 250 were English-speaking and 250 were Spanish-speaking primary care patients. The internal consistency with Cronbach's alpha was measured, and the criterion validity was also assessed by measuring correlations with TOFHLA scores. The result of this study showed the NVS as reliable (Cronbach alpha >0.76 in English and 0.69 in Spanish) and correlates with the TOFHLA (Weiss et al., 2005). The average time required to complete the NVS was 3 minutes. The short time required to perform the assessment in these studies indicates that the NVS is suitable for literacy screening in timelimited clinical settings.

Another study corroborating the efficacy of the Newest Vital Sign (NVS) as a reliable instrument for assessing limited health literacy was conducted by Rowlands et al. (2013), concentrating on the adaptation and validation of the NVS for the UK demographic. The study utilized a three-stage process to adapt and validate the US-developed Newest Vital Sign (NVS) health literacy instrument for the UK population. The NVS-UK was validated against the Test of Functional Health Literacy in Adults (TOFHLA), utilizing Pearson's correlation coefficient (r) and the area under the Receiver Operating Characteristic (ROC) curve, in addition to considering participant educational levels. Validation testing was conducted with 337 participants, and it demonstrated high internal consistency (Cronbach's Alpha = 0.74). Furthermore, validation against the TOFHLA yielded a Pearson's correlation coefficient (r) of 0.49 and an area under the ROC curve of 0.81. The NVS's brevity of 3 minutes makes it suitable for efficient health literacy and it suggests that the NVS is well-suited for literacy screening in clinical settings where time is limited.

Kordovski et al. (2017) studied the usefulness of NVS in determining health literacy among HIV patients. The study tested the NVS against other well-known measures of health literacy, including REALM, and found that the NVS identified more patients with limited health literacy. Kordovski et al. (2017) believe that the NVS evaluates more complex cognitive functions than the other literacy assessments used in the study, which may make it more sensitive to health illiteracy.

In a study by McCune et al. (2016), the NVS was administered to 282 patients in safety net primary care practices. While there was not a significant difference in scores by gender, significant differences were found in regard to education (r=.442, p<.01). A significant difference was also found when differentiated by race (p<.05). Staff and healthcare providers were surveyed regarding the utility of the tool, and overall found it helpful for administering to patients with low health literacy.

Russell et al. (2019) examined whether reliability was affected by administering the NVS over the telephone. Two hundred sixteen patients from a randomized controlled trial were included in the study. Utilizing a different mode of administration did not appear to impact the test-retest properties for the tool. Baseline, in-person, and telephone administration had similar levels of internal consistency (α= 0.76, α=0.76, α=.078), although correlations over time decreased.

The purpose of this pilot study was to explore the feasibility of using the NVS tool to assess health literacy levels in patients at a primary healthcare clinic. The assessment tool consisted of six questions and took less than five minutes to complete. Clinical assessment of health literacy with the NVS will help identify patients with limited health literacy so that educational programs can be customized to meet their level of literacy.

#### Methods

#### Sample and Setting

The design of this study utilized a mixedmethods approach, creating a bi-level data collection model assessing data, including demographics, from two sources: 1) Patients who completed the NVS HL assessment tool and 2) Clinic nursing staff who

completed a brief survey to assess their perception of NVS HL feasibility of use in the clinic. The study was Institutional Review Board approved. All participants were recruited from one primary care clinic in a Southeastern U.S. state. The participants consisted of a convenience sample of 30 patients who attended the clinic. Inclusion criteria were consenting adults between 19 and 65 years old, the ability to speak English, and a scheduled appointment on the day of sampling. Exclusion criteria were lack of cooperation, diminished mental capacity, poor visual acuity, refusal to participate, patients age <19, and those considered too acutely ill to participate. All study subjects were approached to determine initial interest and eligibility to participate. Written informed consent was obtained from all subjects prior to beginning the NVS testing. Once informed consent was obtained, subjects were also given a written demographic questionnaire, which included sex, age, race, and highest level of education.

#### Measures

Health literacy was measured using the NVS, a six-question assessment instrument accompanied by an ice cream nutrition label, which was administered in person. Questions asked as part of the NVS include (1) "If you eat the entire container, how many calories will you eat?" (2) "If you are allowed to eat 60 grams of carbohydrates as a snack, how much ice cream could you have?" (3) "Your doctor advises you to reduce the amount of saturated fat in your diet. You usually have 42 g of saturated fat each day, which includes one serving of ice cream. If you stop eating ice cream, how many grams of saturated fat would you be consuming each day?" (4) "If you usually eat 2,500 calories in a day, what percentage of your daily value of calories will you be eating if you eat one serving?" (5) "Is it safe for you to eat this ice cream?" (6) "Why not?" The test is available in English and Spanish and can be administered in approximately three minutes. For this study, only the English version was used to assess health literacy level. The tool assesses both reading and numeracy skills by requiring the participants to locate and interpret information within the nutrition label. One point was awarded for each correct answer and results were scored after completion. The scores range from zero to six, with four or more correct answers indicating adequate literacy, three indicating marginal literacy, and two or less indicating

inadequate literacy. Nurses and clinic staff who administered the test used a stopwatch to measure the amount of time for each subject to complete the test. To measure the feasibility of use of the NVS in the clinic, clinic nursing staff who administered the test completed an online survey. Questions asked in the survey included (1) "How many years have you been in current practice?" (2) "On average, how many patients enter your facility each week?" (3) "Do you consider the Newest Vital Sign to be a reliable instrument in assessing a patient's health literacy?" (4) "Would you consider using results from the Newest Vital Sign to adjust how you communicate with your patients regarding health and treatment plans?" (5) "What are the potential barriers to implementing the Newest Vital Sign in your current practice?"

#### Procedure

Clinic nursing staff administered the NVS to patients who scheduled an appointment on the day of sampling. A recruiting script describing the study was utilized by clinic nursing staff to ensure uniformity in explaining the study to all participants. At the time of screening, clinic nursing staff approached patients to determine their initial interest and eligibility. After obtaining informed consent, each subject was given a copy of a nutritional label and asked to answer six questions in terms of how they would interpret and use the information. Participants were instructed to complete the NVS on their own, with clinical nursing staff available if they required help with instructions or explanation. Family members and others accompanying subjects were not permitted to assist them in completing the NVS test. After the completion of patient NVS testing, clinic nursing staff were requested to complete the online survey at their leisure. The principal investigator explained that the survey would take approximately 5 minutes of their time. Furthermore, their responses to the questionnaire would remain anonymous and aggregated for statistical reporting and evaluation efforts.

#### Results

#### Analysis

The SAS version 9.2 was used for all data analyses. Descriptive univariate analysis of patientlevel demographic and NVS scores was conducted to examine levels of health literacy in the primary care setting. Chi-square analysis was used to determine statistically significant

differences in NVS score by demographic categories. Timing of the NVS test was reported as mean (standard deviation). Furthermore, qualitative data from the nurses administering the NVS was assessed using similar tests. NVS feasibility was determined by nurses and clinic staffs' affirmative response to a survey regarding the NVS as a reliable instrument to assess health literacy, whether results will be used to modify treatment plans, and barriers to implementation in clinical practice. The survey assessed demographic and practice-related information as well. For all analyses, a p-value of ≤ 0.05 was considered statistically significant. Survey responses and NVS test scores were recorded electronically and transferred automatically to a secured computer database.

Demographic, professional, and practicerelated characteristics of participating healthcare professionals (n = 7) are provided in Table 1. On average, healthcare professionals were 35 years of age, had been practicing for 10.8 years, and worked at a facility that provided treatment for 150 patients per week. All participating healthcare professionals were female (100%) with a majority being black (71.4%) and registered nurses or nurse practitioner students (42.9%, respectively). Seventy-one percent of surveyed healthcare providers considered the NVS to be a reliable instrument, and 85.7% considered using the results from the NVS to adjust treatment plans. The most common barriers to implementing the NVS were patient adherence (85.7%), lack of time to develop or revise treatment plans based on NVS score (57.1%), organizational barriers (42.9%), and other barriers (28.6%, (Figure 1).

A total of 30 patients were administered the NVS. Patients were equally distributed by gender (50% male; 50% female). In general, patients were mostly non-Hispanic black (60%) and had obtained a level of education of at least some college or higher (83.3%). The average time to complete the test was 3.7 minutes. Over half (56.7% ) of patients' NVS scores indicated an adequate level of health literacy, 20% indicated a possibility of limited health literacy, and 23.3% indicated a high likelihood (50% or more) of limited health literacy. Even though patients with a high likelihood of limited health literacy tended to be older (53.7%), female (71.4%), nonHispanic black (85.7%), and took longer to complete the test (4.4 minutes), exam scores did not differ significantly among patient characteristics and demographics (Table 2).

#### Discussion

Measuring literacy levels with the NVS or any other instrument should become a continual process that begins in a primary healthcare setting. The results of the test should be used to tailor patient education to meet each individual's level of understanding in every level of healthcare. This individualized approach to patient education may result in a reduction in healthcare costs and improved patient health outcomes. Patients who are well informed and understand their care plans are empowered to participate in or manage their own healthcare.

 Several factors must be considered when selecting and administering a health literacy screening instrument in the clinical setting. These factors include the cost of the test, staff training, time required to administer and score the test, and whether the test is suitable for the target population. Such factors were identified as barriers by healthcare professionals who administered the NVS test, which could prevent the implementation of the NVS as an instrument to test for health literacy in the clinic setting (Mackert et al., 2016). Possible solutions to facilitate screening include providing reimbursement for use of the assessment and implementing training as part of orientation and ongoing staff development.

Furthermore, the NVS is an efficient health literacy assessment to administer in a busy clinic setting since it takes less than 5 minutes to complete, making it more time efficient compared to other assessments such as the TOFHLA which takes upwards of 20 minutes to complete. The NVS has also been shown to be a more sensitive tool to identify health illiteracy as compared to other health literacy assessments since it applies complex cognitive functions (Kordovski et al., 2017).

The NVS score of less than four indicates limited health literacy and less than two indicates that the patient has a greater than 50% chance of having marginal or inadequate literacy skills. In this study, although over half of the sample population showed an adequate level of health literacy, 43.3% showed either a possibility of limited health literacy or a high

likelihood of limited health literacy. For those identified with limited health literacy, necessary steps should be taken to ensure that health information materials are written at the appropriate levels of comprehension. In this study, a majority of the clinic nursing staff that participated in the survey found the NVS to be a reliable tool to measure health literacy and considered using it to modify their treatment plans. Furthermore, healthcare organizations should educate healthcare professionals to use effective communication and teaching methods so that patients can better understand their care plan and instructions.

#### Limitations

This investigation was a small pilot study utilizing a convenience sampling method and small sample size from a single primary clinic population. Therefore, it may be difficult to form general statements regarding the measure of health literacy levels in all adults with the NVS. However, the results offer some insight into the significance of using the NVS to measure health literacy in primary care settings and support other research that used the NVS to measure health literacy. Our sample took an average of 3.7 minutes to complete the NVS. Factors that contributed to this increased time should be further explored with a larger sample population. Although the survey from healthcare professionals who administered the test was not adequate enough to make general statements concerning the feasibility of the NVS and the need to use its scores to modify treatment plans, the findings support previous studies of the NVS as a brief and reliable instrument for identifying patients at risk for limited health literacy.

#### Conclusion

In conclusion, the study results indicate that the NVS is an objective, reliable, and suitable tool to assess health literacy in less than five minutes. Health literacy cannot be determined by an individual's educational level alone; therefore, some form of assessment will be required. Use of an objective clinical assessment such as the NVS could help identify patients with limited health literacy. Health literacy data in the clinical setting could help tailor education and interventions, which may result in better patient outcomes. The NVS scores will enable clinicians to adjust communication styles in a manner that enhances patients' understanding and

application of health information. The results in this study may stimulate researchers, healthcare organizations, and other stakeholders to generate credible ideas to address individual and systemic barriers to health literacy. This study also encouraged healthcare providers to facilitate health literacy in their interactions with individuals and populations across the nation.

#### Practice Implications

As this study indicated, the majority of clinical nursing staff who administered the NVS assessment considered using the results to adjust patient treatment plans. This implies that the use of the NVS will help identify patients with limited health literacy and promote the tailoring of communication based on the patient's level of comprehension. When patients are found to have limited health literacy, strategies to address the needs of individuals would include clearer communication methods, simple forms, healthcare materials at the appropriate reading level, and teach-back methods.

## Impact of COVID-19 on Recruitment in a Community-Based Breast Cancer Awareness Project

#### Abstract

Black women are less likely to be diagnosed with breast cancer than White women, however they are more likely to die from the disease. Early detection of breast cancer can be accomplished through regular screening mammography, yet disparities have been found in breast cancer screening. Sister Give Me Your Hand (SGMYH) was a grant funded program and a faith-based initiative to recruit lay navigators from churches in New York and New Jersey to assist in recruitment in a breast cancer awareness study and increase the local community's awareness of breast cancer. This mixed methods study planned to use focus groups and to survey women in two cities in New York and New Jersey, however the COVID-19 pandemic outbreak coincided with the implementation of study recruitment. Although the focus groups were held via remote technology, other aspects of the study were not implemented. This experience led the research team to consider the difficulties of recruiting women to internet-based educational programs when the target population of participants may have limited access to connectivity and low digital health literacy. Recommendations to improve this disparity are offered.

#### Introduction

Breast cancer is the second leading cause of cancer related death in women in the United States (Centers for Disease Control and Prevention [CDC], 2023). Although Black women are less likely to be diagnosed with breast cancer, they are more likely to die from the disease (CDC, 2023). Early detection of breast cancer can be accomplished through regular screening mammography, yet disparities have been found in breast cancer screening (Alsheik, 2021). Regardless of ethnicity, race or insurance status, poverty has been found to be the factor that most

negatively affects breast cancer screening rates (Maskarinec et al., 2011; Schootman, et al., 2006). In addition to socioeconomic status, low income women often describe limited access to screening sites, the lack of a usual source of primary care, and transportation difficulties. For these women, other factors such as fear of the procedure and a potential cancer diagnosis, lack of awareness of the need for screening, and competing priorities also affect noncompliance with breast cancer screening (Bea, et al., 2023).

Recruiting Black women into research studies can be difficult. Researchers must consider factors such as fear or suspicion of their motives as well as cultural factors, or family considerations such as a need for childcare or transportation as potential barriers to participation. To successfully engage women to participate in educational programs about breast cancer or other conditions, it is necessary to find and develop relationships with trusted members of the community. In the Black community, churches have been found to be an effective method for recruitment. Churches are often considered central to women's lives, offering spiritual guidance and social support. Churches that have established health ministries may be even more inclined to participate in programs that offer to provide educational resources to the congregation (Whitt-Glover et al., 2016).

Researchers hoping to recruit Black women into studies have often developed relationships with faith-based organizations (FBO) as a means of providing education, outreach, and social support (Marin, et al., 2022). These programs will sometimes train local women to become community health advisors (CHA), who after training can provide a bridge between the community and the health system, working to increase knowledge and awareness and support other women as they try to navigate a complex health care environment.

There have been many outreach programs that have coordinated with FBOs to improve the health of Black women (Newlin et al., 2012). One such program that focused on increasing the rate of cancer screening, was the M.I.C.A.H. Project Heal Program that recruited and educated CHA to bring cancer awareness and prevention knowledge into Black and Latino communities in New York City. What made the M.I.C.A.H. Project Heal program unique was its focus on areas of concern expressed by the FBOs, rather than bringing a set of preconceived goals to the community setting. This program has been successful in collaborating with FBOs by careful planning and recruitment of churches that have pre-existing health ministries and have already proven to be capable of offering health and wellness workshops on various topics. As each church joins the program, congregants are recruited to attend the CHA training programs, and it is these CHA congregants who go on to present the workshops and educate members of the church (Marin, et al., 2022).

In March of 2020, then President Trump declared the COVID-19 pandemic to be a national emergency. In the United States, cities in the northeast were among the first sites to experience the devastation of the pandemic. At that time, local and federal agencies advised that elective procedures such as mammogram screening be cancelled or at least postponed (CDC, 2020). The American College of Radiology (ACR) recommended a temporary discontinuation of mammography, and this led to a major decline in screenings. The outbreak of COVID unfortunately coincided with the initiation of a study to evaluate challenges to breast cancer screening for Black women in two cities in the northeast US.

Sister Give Me Your Hand (SGMYH) was a grant funded program and a faith based initiative that had goals that were similar to that of M.I.C.A.H. Project Heal. Unfortunately, the outbreak of COVID-19 during the recruitment phase of SGMYH forced the research team to change their methods of outreach and recruitment and required a pivot to remote methods of communication to survey and conduct focus groups in two cities in the Northeast. The impact of COVID on FBOs was the major factor that limited the scope and sustainability of the SGMYH program.

#### Methods

The SGMYH plan was to use focus group methodology as the first phase of a mixed methods study. Focus groups, lead by a facilitator using a semi structured interview guide, allow participants to build upon each other's responses, and so can offer researchers much needed information and access to opinions held by group members in a comfortable and convenient setting. Participants recruited for the focus groups would receive \$25 gift cards for participation. This study was reviewed and received Institutional Review Board approval From Cooper University Medical Center.

To facilitate recruitment of women into these focus groups, and to lay the groundwork for educational programs in primarily Black and low income communities, the researchers' plan was to recruit ten churches in two cities, five in New York and five in New Jersey. Each church was asked to recruit two leaders from the congregation to serve as lay navigators and were asked to recruit breast cancer survivors to assist the lay navigators. The navigators were to be paid a one thousand dollar stipend for their effort, which would include attending a 20 hour online navigator training class. The work of the navigators was to develop educational workshops at their churches focused on breast cancer, and to share breast cancer screening and awareness messages with up to 50 women each, with a goal of referring at least ten women from each of the ten churches for screening. In addition to this church-based work, the navigators were expected to refer 30 women to complete a SGMYH survey and five women to participate in focus groups that would discuss barriers and facilitators of breast cancer screening. This project was funded by Pfizer and the American Cancer Society.

With the advent of the COVID-19 pandemic, it was clear to the research team that the initial plan might not work. As the virus spread quickly, specifically in the communities in which recruitment was planned, it became increasingly difficult to engage FBOs in this study. As of April 2020, according to a Pew Research study, among a sample of adults who typically attend services at least once a month, 91% of respondents said that their church had closed its doors to in-person religious services (Gecewicz, 2020). Another study found that by December 2021, although 93% of respondent churches had reopened for services, churches were

experiencing a sharp decline in congregants volunteering to provide support for community services, this despite an increase in community requests for food assistance, spiritual support, and financial help (Banks, 2021). The fear of COVID affected people's willingness to engage with the reopened churches, even if masking was required.

Furthermore, with the rising infection and death toll from the pandemic, the public's priorities shifted from preventive screenings to protecting themselves and their families from COVID. Due to quarantine mandates, churches in both cities moved from in-person services to virtual services. Consequently, the SGMYH navigation program attempted to pivot from an in-person program design to a virtual structure. However, although many churches were able to effectively move to remote services, this change was not as successfully introduced into research recruitment.

#### Results

Although SGMYH program staff pivoted to virtual options to remain engaged with churches, low digital health literacy and the COVID-19 pandemic itself posed a huge barrier to implementing the program. From the Spring of 2020 through the Fall, churches were focused on how to deliver their faithbased messages while also dealing with the realities of the personal impact of COVID. The COVID incidence and mortality rates among Black communities became national news and a very real local problem. The lay navigators were uncomfortable encouraging women to go for screening while conflicting information was being given to the public and local African American communities were confronting large COVID outbreaks and many deaths among friends and family.

Program staff shifted from faith based strategies to community based strategies by engaging local government, non-profit, and civic agencies to assist with recruitment for the research portions of the SGMYH project. The program was ultimately successful in recruiting 33 women for online focus group meetings (sixteen women at the New Jersey location and seventeen in New York City) and 292 to complete the SGMYH survey on barriers and facilitators to mammography screening. However, the women who participated in the focus groups were not necessarily the target population that the

researchers planned to recruit. Of the 33 women in the sample, fourteen women reported having private insurance, which was more than had been expected, and 90% of the women (30 of 33) reported having had a mammogram within the past two years. The results of this study have been published in detail elsewhere (Bea et al., 2023).

Although the researchers were able to conduct five online focus groups using Zoom, the goal of developing sustainable church based health programs with ties to local academic medical centers was not possible during the pandemic. One of the themes identified in the focus group interviews was "getting the word out" and included a discussion of the benefits of working with churches to encourage participation, but an acknowledgment of the fact that this was not possible during COVID. Some of the women interviewed explained that it was likely that the women most in need of breast cancer screening education in their communities were those who did not have access to the internet or who may only be able to use their telephones to access education provided remotely. One participant stated, "A lot of the individuals I work with in my community… they could listen during a phone call, but not (get) online," (Bea, et al., 2023).

#### Discussion

Identified as a public health goal for the 21st century, electronic or digital health literacy is defined as "the ability to seek, find, understand, and appraise health information from electronic sources and apply the knowledge gained to addressing or solving a health problem" ("Digital Health Literacy - a prerequisite competency for future ... - EPHA") (American Society of Clinical Oncology, 2021). The ability to use the internet to learn and potentially solve health problems necessarily requires a high level of health literacy, or the ability to access, understand, and utilize health information to allow for informed decision making (van Kessel et al., 2022). However, individuals with low incomes and less education have been found to have lower levels of health literacy, and low health literacy has been disproportionately linked to members of racial and ethnic minorities and older adults (Muvuka et al., 2020).

It was apparent for this study that required communication and continued outreach with women in low income communities, that poor digital health

literacy would be a barrier, and that perhaps the "old ways" of reaching hard to reach communities may no longer work. Since the pandemic, with increased use of telehealth and internet-based communication between patients and providers, such as patient portals becoming more common, the need to effectively use the internet for health related concerns has only grown. Indeed, Sieck and colleagues (2021) stated that the disparities in digital health literacy will increase existing health disparities and negatively impact social determinants of health.

So how can those of us in research and health care improve digital health literacy? Sieck and colleagues (2021) suggest that health systems partner with community organizations to educate their local populations on digital literacy skills and to facilitate connectivity. Libraries may be a good resource, if available, and community health workers can be recruited into train the trainer programs to improve connectivity and digital skills in local populations. The National Digital Inclusion Alliance (NDIA) is a prime example of an organization that works with grassroots community organizations to increase and improve digital equity across the US (https://www.digitalinclusion.org/policy/). This group prioritizes increasing access to affordable internet service and devices and provides digital skills training and support (NDIA, n.d.).

Going forward, and in line with the goal of improving digital health literacy, it may be appropriate for researchers hoping to recruit populations with low socioeconomic status to continue to utilize remote methodology, as a means of improving digital health literacy. While computer ownership may remain low in low income communities, the percentage of adults in families earning less than \$30,000 a year who use smartphones to access the internet is growing (Vogels, 2021). This is a resource that can be consequential to increasing knowledge and access to health care and improving the recruitment of marginalized communities in research.
