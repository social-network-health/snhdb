# Work-Family Strategies During COVID-19: Examining Gender Dynamics Among Dual-Earner Couples With Young Children

Kristen M. Shockley1 , Malissa A. Clark1 , Hope Dodd1 , and Eden B. King2 <sup>1</sup> Department of Psychology, University of Georgia <sup>2</sup> Department of Psychology, Rice University

There are several existing typologies of dual-earner couples focused on how they dually manage work and family; however, these all assume that couples can outsource childcare during normal work hours and that work is largely conducted outside of the home. Early attempts to control COVID-19 altered these assumptions with daycares/schools closing and the heavy shift to remote work. This calls into question whether couples tended to fall back on familiar gendered patterns to manage work and family, or if they adopted new strategies for the unique pandemic situation. We addressed this question using a sample of 274 dual-earner couples with young children. We content coded couples' qualitative responses about their plans for managing childcare and work commitments and used these codes in a latent class analysis to identify subgroups. Seven classes were identified, with 36.6% of the sample using strategies where women did most or all childcare, 18.9% of the sample using strategies that were not clearly gendered or egalitarian, and 44.5% of the sample using unique egalitarian strategies. We also obtained data from 133 of these couples approximately 7 weeks later regarding their well-being and job performance. Results suggested that women in the *Remote Wife Does It All* class had the lowest well-being and performance. There were nuanced differences between the egalitarian strategies in their relationships with outcomes, with the *Alternating Days* egalitarian category emerging as the overall strategy that best preserved wives' and husbands' well-being while allowing both to maintain adequate job performance.

*Keywords:* work-family, dual-earner couples, division of childcare, gender, remote work

Researchers have noted several typologies of broad strategies that dual-earner couples use to generally manage work and family (e.g., [Hall & MacDermid, 2009;](#page-11-0) [Masterson & Hoobler, 2015;](#page-11-1) [Sweet & Moen, 2006\)](#page-12-0). There are nuanced differences across these typologies, but a common trend is that the most prevalent strategies involve some form of a "neo-traditional" division of labor, where women are primary caretakers inside the home and take on disproportionately more childcare and men work primarily outside the home and their careers are prioritized. This research also assumes that dual-earner couples have the option to outsource childcare during normal work hours and that work is largely conducted outside of the home.

The early stages of the COVID-19 pandemic in the United States introduced an unprecedented context that altered these assumptions. Specifically, institutional and governmental reactions to control the pandemic included the closing of most childcare centers and in-person schools. This sudden substantial increase in childcare responsibilities for dual-earner couples was not necessarily balanced by a reduction in work responsibilities; couples were together confronted with the immediate and ongoing need to *simultaneously* fulfill both work and childcare roles. That is, the division of childcare labor was no longer confined to hours outside of the typical workday, and the boundaries between work and home became blurred for many. Additionally, with the heavy shift to remote work [\(Hickman & Saad, 2020\)](#page-11-2), men were much more physically present in the home than ever before. Physical proximity, and the associated increased salience of family demands, had the potential to markedly shift traditional gendered patterns of behavior.

Despite anecdotal discussion of gendered strategies in the popular press (e.g., [Charlton, 2020;](#page-11-3) [Cohen & Hsu, 2020;](#page-11-4) [Smith, 2020\)](#page-12-1), there are no known empirical tests of what strategies couples are actually using to manage childcare during COVID-19. As such, we do not have knowledge of whether these strategies are highly gendered (as tended to be the case pre-COVID-19) or if new, more egalitarian strategies are being adopted in light of the unique context. Based on these ideas, the goal of the present study was to address the question of whether, in the COVID-19 context, couples are creating novel work-family (WF) management strategies that transcend gender roles or if they are falling back on the familiar patterns seen in previous research during "typical" times. Importantly, we further examined the relationship of these strategies with men and women's job performance and well-being (including health and family functioning). We addressed these questions in a sample of dual-earner heterosexual couples with young children (under age 6) who had stable childcare arrangements pre-COVID-19 but whose childcare arrangements were subsequently disrupted. We surveyed them the week that childcare arrangements were largely suspended across the United States and 6.5– 8.5 weeks later to assess outcomes.

Our research makes several important contributions to the literature. First, in providing basic information about couples' WF management strategies during the pandemic and how these strategies link to key outcomes, we hope to answer calls to examine WF situations in times of crisis [\(Eby et al., 2015\)](#page-11-5) and to contribute to the literature about best practices during such times. The COVID-19 pandemic can be considered a crisis situation, defined as a "low-probability, high impact event" that is characterized by ambiguity, uncertainty regarding means of resolution, and significant consequences [\(Yu et al., 2008\)](#page-12-2). Given the crisis context, one cannot assume prior typologies of couples' WF management strategies would mirror current findings. Crisis situations may have discontinuous effects on individuals and couples [\(Eby et al., 2015\)](#page-11-5) and reveal new, complex associations between division of labor and outcomes. If we find that certain strategies are better in this crisis situation than others for well-being and performance, couples may gain insight into how they may adjust their own strategies moving forward during the pandemic, in other crisis situations in the future (e.g., natural disasters), or even in more minor situations once COVID-19 has subsided (e.g., child is sick for a few days and must stay home).

Second, our study empirically answers anecdotal questions about gender and childcare during the pandemic. By examining the gender dynamics within couples' WF management strategies, we provide important evidence that has implications for understanding (a) potential downstream career consequences for men and women and (b) the extent to which the gendered separate spheres of work and home are fixed versus adaptive to crisis. With regard to the former point, if women take on the bulk of childcare duties during the pandemic, this could be a leading indicator of backtracking in women's progression in organizations. With regard to the latter point, understanding what happens when work and home are in the same sphere for both men and women offers a unique lens into the malleability of the gendered division of household labor.

A third contribution of our study is linking dual-earner WF management strategies to well-being and performance outcomes. There is a surprising dearth of research on this topic generally. Although we focus on short-term implications in the context of a pandemic, our findings may be able to guide research outside the pandemic as well (e.g., short-term relationship tension stemming from a particular WF management strategy may manifest in more stable attitudes such as marital dissatisfaction if that strategy is adopted long-term). Furthermore, short-term implications are important in their own right as they can have meaningful downstream effects on individual well-being (e.g., lack of sleep has been linked to obesity, hypertension, and diabetes; [Altman et al., 2012\)](#page-11-6), relationship outcomes (e.g., tension from this situation may simmer and resentment may build between spouses), and workplace outcomes (e.g., companies may use the current situation to make long-term decisions such as promotions or raises).

# **Dual-Earner Couples' Work-Family Management Strategies**

There are numerous extant typologies of dual-earner couples in the literature and they vary in the focal variable used to categorize couples, including work and family salience [\(Yogev & Brett,](#page-12-3) [1985\)](#page-12-3), family identity [\(Masterson & Hoobler, 2015\)](#page-11-1), career decisions [\(Becker & Moen, 1999;](#page-11-7) [Pixley, 2008\)](#page-12-4), hours in childcare, household labor, and/or paid employment of each spouse [\(Hall &](#page-11-0) [MacDermid, 2009;](#page-11-0) [Kitterød & Lappegård, 2012;](#page-11-8) [Sweet & Moen,](#page-12-0) [2006\)](#page-12-0). Common themes across typologies include some version of the following: (a) both people are highly committed to work and to family demands, and may rely on specific strategies such as outsourcing, reducing performance standards, or reducing sleep time, (b) asymmetrical strategies that involve one person devoting more to work (family) while the other devotes more time to the other domain, (c) and one person taking on the bulk of family labor while not scaling back at work. The latter two strategies are highly gendered—it is usually the wife who scales back work or takes on a "second shift"—and they represent a large proportion of couples in most studies.

Turning to the COVID-19 context, we draw from multiple theoretical lenses to inform why potential WF management strategies may be highly gendered as found in previous work, or may depart from this pattern. Numerous theories have been put forth to explain the gendered division of labor (cf. [Shockley & Shen,](#page-12-5) [2016\)](#page-12-5); we hone in on theories that focus on gender itself rather than factors that correlate with gender (e.g., relative resources, economic theories of specialization). The "doing gender" [\(West &](#page-12-6) [Zimmerman, 1987,](#page-12-6) [2009\)](#page-12-7) perspective argues that due to gender role socialization women (men) view domestic (paid) labor as an act that affirms femininity (masculinity), which contributes to the asymmetrical patterns. Similarly, [Blair-Loy \(2003\)](#page-11-9) asserts couples rarely question the idea that the wife rather than the husband should cut back on work to care for children because of internalized cultural schemas of women's family devotion [\(Eagly, 1987;](#page-11-10) [Haines et al., 2016\)](#page-11-11). Additionally, marital power theories [\(Rollins](#page-12-8) [& Bahr, 1976\)](#page-12-8) argue that the person with more power in a marriage prevails in situations of conflicting goals (e.g., who will sacrifice work when childcare is necessary). Holding other factors (e.g., relative resources) constant, gender (being male) is a predictor of power in part due to hegemonic masculinity embedded in society (e.g., [Lukes, 2005;](#page-11-12) [Messerschmidt, 2018\)](#page-11-13). There is little reason to suspect that COVID-19 would fundamentally alter any of these aforementioned processes, suggesting that WF management strategies during COVID-19 mimic the gendered strategies commonly used before the pandemic. Indeed, a rare study of WF strategies during a crisis [\(Sigurdardottir & Garðarsdóttir, 2018\)](#page-12-9) suggests a gendered trend. Researchers found that Icelandic men took shorter parental leave after the 2008 economic recession compared to the preceding 4 years, although the opposite trend was observed for women, who took longer leaves.

On the other hand, characteristics unique to COVID-19 invoke other theoretical concepts that introduce the potential for new strategies. The notion that men and women can and should be relegated to (or maintain primary responsibility for) work and home roles, respectively, is deeply ingrained in social norms around the globe (e.g., the concept of "separate spheres"; [Davis &](#page-11-14) [Greenstein, 2009;](#page-11-14) [Kerber, 1988\)](#page-11-15). Yet the situational demands imposed by the pandemic have the potential to disrupt those norms for a variety of reasons. First, the restriction of movement imposed by the pandemic meant that work and home were no longer separate spaces; the sharp increase in remote work resulted in a sharp decrease in the physical boundaries between work and home. This meant that many men no longer had the option of leaving home for work and were thus physically embedded with their families. Meal preparation and emotional and physical needs of children may have been more immediately salient for men than ever before.

Second, the pandemic confronted couples with a necessary and urgent decision-making context often involving substantial changes to *both* members' work arrangements. This context is meaningfully different from previously studied decisions, such as whether one partner should take a new job or which partner is responsible for picking up a sick child, because it explicitly involves *both* partners' work *and* home lives. The possibility that work and home could be separate spheres for men and women may have been completely disrupted by the pandemic and thus allow for noticeable change.

Third, the double ABCX model of family stress and adaptation [\(Hill, 1958;](#page-11-16) [McCubbin & Patterson, 1983\)](#page-11-17) explicitly focuses on how families adapt to crisis situations. The model is complex, but a core idea is that the successful coping of families to crisis situations depends in part on their ability to accept shifting role patterns and expectations [\(Hill, 1958\)](#page-11-16). A key takeaway relevant to the present context is that many couples do substantially shift their roles in reaction to crisis situations rather than rely on previous defaults.

Based on these varying perspectives, we pose the following as a research question:

*Research Question 1:* In the COVID-19 context, are couples using unique, new WF management strategies or are they falling back on the familiar gendered patterns found in previous research?

# **Outcomes of Couples' Work-Family Management Strategies**

As noted above, there is very little research linking general couple-level WF management strategies to well-being or job performance outcomes. However, some evidence can be gleaned from the related general division of labor literature. Research on uneven division of family labor is quite mixed in terms of marital quality and the relationship seems to be moderated by perceptions of fairness, such that marital quality decreases for the person doing more labor if he or she feels it is unfair (cf. [Shockley & Shen,](#page-12-5) [2016\)](#page-12-5). In terms of health outcomes, psychological distress has received the most attention, with the bulk of studies finding that couples who divide paid and family labor in an egalitarian manner experience the lowest distress. Lastly, there is some evidence when women's careers are prioritized or they earn more than men (e.g., a "non-traditional" division of paid labor) both men and women have poorer health outcomes (cf. [Shockley & Shen, 2016\)](#page-12-5). In summary, the trend seems to be that more egalitarian strategies lead to most favorable outcomes at least for health and marital outcomes, although there are some nuances to these findings based on the nature of the nonegalitarian strategy and gender.

In the present context, we decided to focus on well-being in the family, health, and job performance domains for a holistic assessment. Rather than focusing on more enduring attitudes such as marital satisfaction, we examined marital relationship tension which may vary more on a short-term basis [\(Birditt et al., 2017\)](#page-11-18). We also examined family cohesion, with the idea that strategies chosen during COVID-19 could impact the family as a whole rather than just the couple. In terms of health, we focused on psychological distress given the precedent in the literature, as well as sleep hours. We thought sleep hours were particularly important during the pandemic because of the time crunch with added childcare, resulting in potential cuts to sleep. Finally, in order to see the job-related implications, we used two indicators of selfreported job performance (general job performance and job performance relative to pre-COVID-19) over the study period. For each outcome, we examined the wives' and husbands' reports separately. For variables that were individual in nature (performance and health variables) we also calculated a summed variable to represent well-being and performance across the couple.

*Research Question 2:* To what extent do the respective WF strategies relate to wives' and husbands' family functioning (relationship tension, family cohesion), health (sleep hours and psychological distress), and job performance?

### **Method**

### **Participants**

Participants were 334 married couples living in the United States (668 individuals). In order to be eligible, couples had to meet the following criteria at the Time 1 survey: Both spouses worked full-time (-32 hr per week) and were still required to work (whether remote or not) during sheltering in place orders. The couple had to have at least one child under age 6 with regular daytime childcare unavailable (e.g., daycare was closed, nanny could not come). We further screened out 35 couples because we suspected that their responses were from a single person rather than an actual couple1 , and 16 additional couples because their responses to the open-ended questions revealed they did not meet the study criteria (i.e., unmarried or not working, *n* 3), or their open-ended responses were too terse or indecipherable (*n* 13). Lastly, we removed 9 couples in same sex marriages.2 Therefore, we had usable data from 274 couples at Time 1.

# **Measures**

All items are listed in the [Appendix.](#page-12-10) All items were completed by both spouses. For Time 2 measures participants were asked to reflect on the period since they took the first survey.

# *Plan for Managing Childcare and Work Commitments (Time 1)*

Participants responded to three open-ended questions created for the study to assess the couple's plan for dealing with work and childcare: (a) "What is you and your spouse's plan for dealing with both of your work commitments during the upcoming period when your normal childcare is disrupted? Please be as detailed as possible." (b) "How will you have to adjust your work role during this time?" and (c) "How will your spouse have to adjust his/her work role during this time?"

### *Implementation of Plan (Time 2)*

Participants were first reminded of their verbatim response from Time 1 regarding their intended plan. They were then asked to rate how well they followed that plan. Responses were set on a 10 point Likert scale ranging from *did not follow the plan at all* to *followed the plan perfectly.*

### *Family Functioning (Time 2)*

*Family cohesion* was assessed using [Huffman et al.'s \(2017\)](#page-11-21) 3-item measure and *marital relationship tension* was assessed using three items from [Matthews et al.'s \(2006\).](#page-11-22) Responses were set on a 5-point *strongly disagree* to *strongly agree* Likert scale.

#### *Health*

*Sleep quantity* was assessed by asking "During this time period, on average, how many hours of actual sleep did you get at night?" *Psychological distress* was assessed with [Kessler et al.'s \(2002\)](#page-11-23) 10-item measure. Responses were set on a 5-point Likert scale ranging from *none of the time* to *all of the time.*

#### *Job Performance*

First, participants completed a four-item measure from [Abramis \(1994\).](#page-11-24) Responses were set on a 5-point Likert scale ranging from *very poorly* to *exceptionally well.* Second, we assessed the extent to which participants felt they had met their work responsibilities during this period using a study-created item: "During the pandemic many parents have not been able to meet 100% of their work responsibilities. ### *Attention Checks*

Five items were used to measure careless responding and were presented randomly throughout the surveys (e.g., "I do not understand a word of English"). Responses were set on a 5-point Likert scale that ranged from *strongly disagree* to *strongly agree.* Responses were deemed incorrect if they did not select one of two response options reflecting the correct level of agreement (e.g., *strongly disagree* or *disagree* in the example).

Scores across the couple (health, psychological distress, general and relative job performance) were computed by summing the individual scores.

# **Results**

### **Coding of Stated Plans**

We followed an inductive process in generating codes for the descriptions of plans for managing childcare and work commitments. We reviewed the wife's and husband's reports in tandem to provide a holistic assessment of the plans. Two of the study authors each reviewed a unique subset of the data (20% each) and independently generated common categories that emerged from the descriptions as well as different codes within each category. These categories were compared and refined. Next, all four of the study authors reviewed a separate randomly selected subset of the data (10%) and attempted to code using the initial code sheet. It was agreed upon that substantial refinements to the coding sheet were necessary. The coders met to discuss these refinements and then formally coded the subset. Agreement was 89.3%. All instances where coders were not in perfect agreement were discussed and further minor clarifications and revisions were made to the coding sheet. We then moved forward in having two coders per description. Agreement across cases and categories was 74.8%. In all cases where there was a coding disagreement a third coder was assigned. In 89.5% of these cases, the new coder chose one of the existing codes and we selected the majority code. In the remaining 10.5%, a fourth coder reviewed the codes and a majority code emerged.

# **Analyses**

We address Research Question 1 in two ways. First, in [Table 2,](#page-5-0) we present the distinct categories that emerged from the data and the prevalence of various alternatives within each category. Next, in order to determine if certain patterns across categories emerged within the data we conducted a latent class analysis (LCA), a technique which identifies latent subgroups based on individual responses from multivariate categorical data [\(Rindskopf, 2009\)](#page-12-11).3 We conducted the LCA using Mplus 7.4 [\(Muthén & Muthén,](#page-11-25) [2015\)](#page-11-25) and began by specifying one profile and increased the number of profiles until fit no longer substantially improved. Following best practice recommendations (e.g., [Gabriel et al.,](#page-11-26) [2018\)](#page-11-26), we determined fit by looking for lower values with the Akaike information criterion (AIC), Bayesian information criterion (BIC), consistent AIC (C-AIC) and sample-size-adjusted BIC, higher values for entropy, and significant values for the LoMendell-Rubin likelihood ratio test and the bootstrap likelihood ratio test. The results of the LCA are shown in [Table 3.](#page-6-0) As there are no clear criteria for LCA fit indices, we looked at the various indicators holistically, prioritizing those in which the BIC and C-AIC values were lowest, to determine the best factor solution (e.g., [Peugh & Fan, 2013\)](#page-12-12). The 7-factor solution has the lowest BIC, C-AIC, and AIC; thus, we proceeded with 7 factors.

[Table 4](#page-7-0) shows the descriptive labels given to each class, representative quotes, and the proportion of couples in that class that were assigned a particular code for each category. This gives a sense of what the modal strategies across categories were for each of the seven classes.

With regard to Research Question 1, several of the strategies reflected gendered patterns. Specifically, two of the strategies— *Wife Remote and Does it All* and *Wife Remote and Husband Pitches in on Occasion ("Wife Remote Doing Most"*)— were clearly gendered and included 31.8% of the sample. The *Husband Not Remote and Outsourcing Fully or Partially Outsourcing With Wife Filling in Gap* also had a gendered component for 36% of the couples in this class, or 4.8% of the total sample. Thus, 36.6% of the total sample used traditional gendered strategies.

Other categories emerged that have been noted in some previous work as couple WF management strategies, albeit less common ones, include *Wife Not Remote and Staggering Shiftwork or Husband Does Childcare* (10.4%). *Outsourcing Fully* (64% of 13.3% 8.5%) has been mentioned in some conceptual work [\(Masterson & Hoobler, 2015\)](#page-11-1), but it seems fairly unique during COVID-19 given the higher reliance on friends or family than formal paid childcare.

Three unique egalitarian categories emerged—*Alternating Working Days While Not Remote and Reducing Hours ("Alternating Days"*), *Egalitarian Remote Workers With Planned Minishifts ("Remote Mini-shifts"), Egalitarian Remote Workers With Daily Need-based Alternation Without Reducing Work Hours ("Remote Need-Based Alternating").* Together, these comprised 44.5% of the sample. Thus, in response to Research Question 1 there was a mix of unique and "typical" strategies.

Before running these analyses, we trimmed the data to only those who completed the Time 2 survey (*n* 172). # **Discussion**

In this study, we sought to examine if the strategies dual-earner couples are using to meet their competing work and childcare demands during COVID-19 reflect progress toward a more egalitarian division of labor or remain highly gendered, and the link between strategy choice and a range of outcomes. Even though the separation of the spheres of work and home had largely disintegrated, and men were much more likely to be physically present in their homes, we found evidence a substantial portion of couples were engaging in highly gendered strategies. This most commonly involved wives working remotely and taking on all childcare with little adjustment to husbands' work roles, as well as similar scenarios wherein husbands pitched in with childcare occasionally (e.g., one day a week or during an important meeting for the wife). Even when some outsourcing was available, the remainder of childcare mostly fell on wives. This evidence confirms anecdotal reports of the persistent gendered division of labor during COVID-19.

Further concerning is that for many of the outcomes, women in the most lopsided and common category (*Wife Remote and Does It All*) experienced the least favorable outcomes of all classes, including lowest family cohesion, highest relationship tension, and lowest job performance by both metrics. This is somewhat a departure from research described previously, where results were not consistently poorer for those doing all the labor. It may be due in part to the fact that doing *all* of the childcare equates to many more childcare hours overall than when childcare was outsourced during work hours. Men in these couples were not immune to negative outcomes, as they also experienced the lowest family cohesion and highest relationship tension. Although their work situations seemed minimally altered, job performance was rather low compared to many of the other classes as well. Perhaps these men are less focused at work due to marital strife resulting from the increased WF burdens falling on their wives.

There are several practical implications of these findings. With regards to the counterintuitive performance findings, it is important for companies to understand that withholding flexibility from parents affects the entire couple—not just the member who picks up the slack. From a societal standpoint, these results raise serious concerns about potential setbacks in women's career advancement. Moreover, for couples who find themselves in a situation where the wife can work remote and the husband cannot, our findings suggest that they will fare considerably better if the husband actively provides some relief by taking an occasional day off or cutting hours short to support his remote partner. In fact, both husbands and wives in the *Wife Remote and Doing Most* class had significantly better relationship outcomes and relative job performance compared to those in the *Wife Remote and Doing It All* class.

Two of these (*Remote Minishifts*, *Remote Need-Based Alternating*) occurred when both members of the couple were working remotely. This lends support to the idea that the removal of separate spheres and physical embeddedness of men in family spaces may facilitate equality. Moreover, although these two strategies appear similar on the surface, they differ in regard to the specific manner in which couples were trading off watching children. In one class, partners scheduled minishifts (e.g., one person works mornings and the other works afternoons). With this process came a natural reduction in work hours for both partners for many members of this class. In the other class, scheduling was not as rigid and varied based on children's needs and partners' immediate work obligations and work hours were not reduced. Husbands in the *Remote Minishifts* class reported significantly lower relative job performance than the *Remote Need-Based Alternating* and these couples had the lowest couple-level relative job performance of all classes. This study provides empirical support for implementation of the *Outsourcing Fully* WF management strategy, which heretofore has been primarily conceptual [\(Masterson & Hoobler, 2015\)](#page-11-1).

From a practical standpoint, our findings suggest that although it varies somewhat by specific outcome, *Alternating Days* may be the best strategy for couples, especially those who are not working remotely. It preserves well-being while maintaining adequate levels of performance. Speculatively, by virtue of alternating, both people in these couples were essentially able to keep work and family segmented, which has been linked to better health and performance outcomes (e.g., [Kinnunen et](#page-11-30) [al., 2016;](#page-11-30) [Smit et al., 2016\)](#page-12-14). Also intertwined in strategy choice is the location of work. Many workers were required to work remotely due to state mandated closures, and in that case, were less likely to adopt the *Alternating Days* strategy. Given projections that location flexibility is here to stay [\(Ozimek, 2020\)](#page-11-31) many couples may find themselves both working remotely in the future. In this case when both people are working remotely, our results suggest that couples would be advised to adopt a more flexible approach (e.g., *Remote Need-Based Alternating*) rather than rigid minishift schedule. It is unclear which mechanisms are driving the differences. Speculatively, it may be enhanced communication that this strategy requires, the fact that couples are more responsive to each other's needs and that reduces marital tension (indeed there was a trend for lower marital tension in the *Remote Need-Based Alternating* class), or having a shared meaning of the situation—a key component the Double ABCX theory (e.g., [Weber, 2011\)](#page-12-15). Future research would benefit from testing these mechanisms to derive even more specific recommendations.

Our results must be interpreted in light of a few limitations. First, our sample had a high average income, drawing into question its broader generalizability to lower income workers. We also used self-report measures of job performance, which can be prone to inflation [\(DeNisi & Murphy, 2017\)](#page-11-32). Third, we were focused on the very narrow context of COVID-19. However, we believe our results still have important implications for couples in other situations that share some features of the pandemic, including extreme weather where schools and daycares may close but workplaces still require remote work, situations where children have a disability or chronic illness and cannot attend typical childcare arrangements, or even parents facing long daycare waitlists (e.g., [Shellenbarger, 2010\)](#page-12-16) and must manage work with an infant at home.

In closing, our research presents a rare glimpse into how dualearner couples manage childcare during a crisis situation when the typical fallbacks are not available. The results suggest that, even in the context of an unprecedented crisis when the spheres of work and home were overlapping for men and women, gendered patterns of childcare responsibilities persist. It has been argued that the conditions of the pandemic offered an opportunity for fathers to engage more fully with their family roles and to shift the balance in gender role expectations. Instead, the data presented here suggest that the burden of intensive, unexpected, and ongoing childcare responsibilities is disproportionately falling on mothers. Yet when parents did create more novel and egalitarian strategies for managing childcare in the crisis, particularly by alternating work days, their performance and well-being were preserved. Such novel solutions may be precisely those that help families and organizations move forward in a post-COVID reality.
