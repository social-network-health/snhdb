#### **Abstract**

We propose a new method for analyzing and visualizing information on a large collection of personal networks to uncover the socio-centric structure of relationships among aggregated actors that we clustered into categories. The network of categories identifies the links between groups of sampled ego actors sharing a given attribute (e.g., "being immigrant organizations") and abstract attribute-based groups or types of alters (e.g., "any immigrant organization"). This method takes advantage of both whole networks and egocentered networks analytical approaches since it gains insights into the context's whole network structure while generalizing the results to a wider population. The method can be applied to data obtained through modified versions of instruments such as position or resource generators, which are easy to collect within standard large-N representative surveys. ## Introduction

Whole networks and egocentric networks are usually considered two different approaches to social network analysis (SNA) that address two different layers of the social world (McCarty et al. 2019). Few techniques combine elements of both in a single method. This article proposes a method to merge the two traditions by analyzing egocentric network data as a whole network structure. This method of building whole networks from collections of egocentric networks combines the strengths of both approaches: It enables the visualization and analysis of the overall structure of relationships in a given field while using a representative sample of actors' egocentric networks, thus allowing for the statistical inference of the results.

Previous efforts to merge the two approaches have developed methods to overlap egonetworks into a whole sociocentric network by identifying all the interviewees and their referrals and linking up information about their connections (Hancean et al. 2021 ˆ ; Mouw et al. 2014). Either simulations on egocentric networks are used to compute estimates of the underlying whole network (Smith 2012, 2015; Verdery et al. 2017), or personal networks are summarized into single graphs of relationships across categories or classes (Barranco et al. 2019; Brandes et al. 2008). However, these methods require either long and complex link-tracing, sampling-based data-collection procedures or information on alter–alter ties, which are costly and hard to collect. Our method aggregates ego–alter relationships into a whole network–like construct based solely on a modified version of the resource generator (RG), which is a simple and easy way to collect ego–alter relationships. Unlike name generators and name interpreters, the simplicity of the network data on which the method is based makes it a powerful tool, as it enhances its chances to be included as a module in large-N representative samples.

This method is based on the transformation of the classes or abstract attributive categories of ego and alter actors into the nodes of a whole network. By "categories," we refer to node attributes exogenously defined. In our empirical research, we identified categories as types of organizational actors based on distinctions between civil-society organizations and branches of the administration and the interests they speak for, such as trade unions, business actors, immigrant actors, or welfare (charity) organizations, to name a few (see Table 1 in the Methodology section for the complete list). Other theory-based classifying methods could be applied, if they are consistent and applicable to both ego and alter actors.

Network ties are then measured as the average strength of the interaction (such as frequency or intensity of collaboration or support) between these groups of egos and alters clustered according to the same attribute method. In this article, we apply this method to a piece of research on the political representation of immigrants' interests in local governance networks. The aim is to find out how organizations that represent immigrant interests are embedded in networks of collaboration among local organizational actors across different policy fields. The empirical material stems from a survey conducted with local organizational actors in 40 German and French cities. The results of this application illustrate the usefulness of the methodological construct. We conclude by discussing the strengths and limitations of the method, as well as envisaging other potential applications of it.

# Ego Networks and Whole Networks

Social network theory and analysis focus on the study of relationships between actors or social entities, and on the resulting structure of connections. There are two main distinct approaches to collecting and analyzing social network data: ego networks and whole networks.

On the one hand, ego or egocentric network analysis studies the social networks built among the contacts that are linked to a focal actor we call "ego." This allows a deeper understanding of the "personal community" within which the actors under study are embedded (Fischer 1982). Egocentric analysis can study the network composition and the resources exchanged through the network. Moreover, when information on the relationship between the alters surrounding the ego is also collected, we can study the connectedness of the network—i.e., the formal properties of the network structure such as its density, the existence of subgroups, the intermediation of particular alters, and the like. One strength of this approach is that it connects with traditional attribute-based probability sampling statistics, as it collects network data on a set of independent actors—or applies multilevel analysis techniques to account for the nested structure of alter-level and ego-level egocentered network measures (Wellman and Frank 2001). The properties of the networks are attributed to ego, and this can then be used as a variable in standard statistical analysis (Crossley et al. 2015; McCarty et al. 2019). Therefore, the characteristics of the analyzed networks can be extrapolated to a broader population.

The main limitation of this approach is that it focuses on the flow of dyadic or first-degree relationships from and to ego. For example, when studying immigrant personal or organizational ego networks, research tends to focus on the question of whom do immigrant actors collaborate with, and who collaborates with immigrant actors (see Lubbers et al. 2010; Schiller et al. 2020). As a result, it cannot consider how a broader structure or patterns of *Bolíbar et al.* 113 4 Field Methods 0(0)

relationship among non-immigrant actors might constrain the networks of immigrant organizations.

On the other hand, sociocentric or whole network analysis studies the patterns of relationships between all pairs of actors in a defined and bounded group, community, or context. Although it only considers actors within that given setting, it allows the larger social network structure thereby built to be considered. This includes not only first-order contacts, as in egocentric networks, but also the higher-order neighbors of the actors studied. That information is crucial to conducting a deeper analysis of the formal structure of the entire network. This includes meso-level network elements, such as clusters, triads, or components, or, on the node level, the structural positions and locations of each individual actor within the network. This approach allows one of the premises of SNA to be assessed, namely that the network structure matters—i.e., that the broader shape and structure of a whole network, beyond the particular relationships built by a given actor, affect the dyadic relationships in which that actor might engage (Degenne and Forse´ 1999). However, this approach is mostly applied on a limited number of case studies, with consequent limitations on statistical inference and the generalization of results. Moreover, missing data and incomplete information hinder the calculation of whole network measures. As a result, it is often practically impossible to collect full network data on many populations of interest. In particular, reconstructing inter-organizational whole networks in different cities is a challenging endeavor that requires a lot of time and resources, often resulting in network maps that pose serious concerns on the potential impact of non-responses.

Efforts to overcome the gap between whole networks and ego-centric approaches often have limitations and burdensome requirements for data collection. We can identify three techniques for reconstructing whole networks using ego-network samples.

A first way of doing this is to identify the particular egos and alters to connect the egocentric networks in which sampled egos nominate each other or share the same alters. A whole network is always a sum of ego networks, and the adjacency matrix can be created by adding information on the relationships of all network members. However, it is often hard to retrieve information from all network members, since a list of their components might not be available or they might be hard to reach. Therefore, a novelty in this approach stems from combining ego networks retrieved through a link-tracing sampling design in which the alters of the egos are subsequently surveyed through several waves of recruitment to reveal further nodes and connections. Following respondent-driven sampling (RDS) logics, the recruitment stops when an equilibrated sample composition and size is reached. As a result, a whole network can be reconstructed in hidden and even multi-sited populations (see Molina et al. 2022). However, this method requires complex, long, and sometimes tedious fieldwork (Hancean et al. 2021 ˆ ) to identify each individual while maintaining participants' anonymity and confidentiality.

A second type of method relies on simulation techniques for inferring the structural characteristics of the whole network based on the information provided by ego-network samples. These simulation-based methods allow the contextual network effects to be estimated (Smith 2012, 2015) but need rich ego-network data to compute quality simulations. Smith's method (2012, 2015), for instance, requires information on the size of the true whole network, the size of personal networks, the attributes of egos and alters, and alter–alter ties. Verdery et al.'s method (2017) requires information on the referring chain structure within an RDS sampling scheme plus additional insights into the overlap between the referee's and referral's networks.

Our method is much simpler and more intuitive, as we do not aim to reconstruct the actual whole network of actors but a network representing the structuring of relationships across the attributes or actor types.

The network of categories is inspired by clustered graphs (Brandes et al. 2008). Clustered graphs are a method of analyzing collections of ego networks summarizing the structure of the alter–alter ties in which egos are embedded (see Brandes et al. 2008). Their method is also based on the analysis of relationships between classes, not individual nodes. They calculate the size and connectivity between subgroups or clusters of alters defined by sharing a given attribute. The method can visualize a large volume of egocentric networks simultaneously in the visualization of a single whole network-like graph, thereby analyzing simultaneously the structure and composition of networks. See Lubbers et al. (2010) and Bol´ıbar et al. (2015) as applications of this method. However, to know how the categories are connected to each other, clustered graphs also require information to be collected about how the particular alters ego relates to and their attributes, as well as the even more time-consuming effort of retrieving alter–alter ties. Unlike Brandes et al.'s (2008) clustered graphs, in our method, the collection of networks is not superposed into a whole network using alter–alter ties and only shows egoalter ties, which enables the use of star-shaped ego-networks without information on the alter–alter network.

Our method could be described as a new tool to analyze personal network data by applying Brandes et al.'s (2008) clustered graphs' logics into the family of short egocentric network instruments based on network composition (see Marsden and Hollstein [2023] for a review), such as resource generation– (Van Der Gaag & Snijders, 2005) or position generation–(Lin et al. 2001) inspired tools. Instead of asking about social relationships with particular people, resource and position generators have been widely administered to inquire about links to social locations, measuring thus access to specific resources or to positions of the occupational ladder, respectively (Lin and Erickson 2008). Our method could also use aggregated relational data (ARD) *Bolíbar et al.* 115 6 Field Methods 0(0)

(McCormic 2020), an instrument used to uncover personal network size by asking about the number of contacts respondents have with particular subpopulations. Those instruments have to be thoroughly adapted to the research context, but they involve relatively simple data collection procedures as they make modest demands on survey respondents: They do not require knowing about either the relationship ego establishes with specific alters (using name generators), nor the attributes of all those alter-nodes (using name interpreters), which pose a serious burden on respondents and are strongly affected by sensitive decisions about the limitation of the number of nodes considered.

Similarly, to reconstruct the network of categories, we asked directly about the relationship of organizational egos with organizations of the categories of interest—e.g., "Do you reach out to immigrant associations?" or "Do you reach out to political parties?" referring here to any immigrant association or any political party, respectively.

The result of the network of categories also resembles affiliation networks—i.e., two-mode networks representing relationship between classes or types of organizations based on common individual actors' memberships (as in Diani's [2009] civil society networks). However, the network of categories is not based on affiliation network data. The relationships between categories or types are based on direct information on the interaction between organizations of those types (retrieved through modified RGs), not simply on the identification of co-memberships or on more or less refined ways of accounting for co-occurrence of attributes in actors (see, e.g., Barranco et al.'s [2019] or Escobar and Tejero's [2018] analysis of coincidences among attribute-based classes).

# Example of Application: Have Immigrant Organizations Entered Local Governance Networks?

The representation of immigrants in local policy making has become a thriving field of research. While much literature has discussed the inclusion of immigrants in parliaments, other forms of immigrant representation in policy making exist and have become more important over the years. Approaches to immigrant political representation need to consider patterns of interaction between state and non-state organizations as they are "central components of urban governance" (McQuarrie and Marwell 2009:250). Governance is assumed to become increasingly important in the process of policy making (Rhodes 1997). It involves the interaction between public and nonpublic actors and the complex processes and negotiations involved to achieve a certain service or policy outcome (Klijn 2008).

Our research focuses on the embeddedness of organizations such as immigrant councils, umbrella organizations of immigrant associations, and associations that represent immigrants' rights in local governance networks in Germany and France. We investigate whether and to what extent these immigrant representatives interrelate and cooperate with organizational actors at the local level, including administrative, political, and economic actors, trade unions, welfare organizations, and representatives of other dimensions of urban diversity, such as women, LGBTQI+ and youth associations, and religious representatives, to name but a few. We want to find out how immigrant representatives are positioned within the broad range of actors that influence city policies related to a diversifying population.

We also want to know whether organizations that represent immigrant interests are embedded in governance networks across different policy fields. Given that local administrations have actively pursued strategies of "diversity mainstreaming" in recent years, we want to investigate whether this has broadened the range of spaces for political representation, as well as the policy fields in which immigrant organizations take part, beyond what Bousetta (1997:221) calls "a local integration field." With this concept, he refers to "a specific dimension of the local political space" that addresses ethnic minority issues and the challenges that ethnic diversity brings to cities. Mainstreaming refers to a shift in the target groups of policies, from a specific group in society (i.e., immigrants) to society as a whole. It also refers to a change in the focus and attention of policies. Mainstreaming is in place when specific immigrant policies and institutional structures for immigrant incorporation are dissolved or become irrelevant because the attention and personal resources with which to consider immigrant concerns is integrated or mainstreamed across policy fields (Scholten and van Breugel 2017).

Since governance takes place through interactions and networks, SNA allows us to capture the relational character of local politics, and the presence of actors representing immigrant interests within these networks.

## **Methodology**

#### Data

We present findings from the CITYDIV project (Moutselos et al., 2020), a large comparative project that investigates local organizational actors' responses to the increasing diversity of urban populations in French and German cities. The project was conducted at the Max Planck Institute for the Study of Religious and Ethnic Diversity by an interdisciplinary team of scholars. We conducted a survey with relevant urban organizations in the Germany's 20 largest cities (except for the city-states of Berlin, Hamburg, and Bremen in Germany)<sup>1</sup> and the 20 largest cities in France (except for Paris, which is the only city that is also a *département*).<sup>2</sup>

through a multiple-step theory-based procedure (i.e., guided by our theoretical perspective and assumptions). Through desk research, we first identified the relevant urban actors involved in diversity-related policies and then, to make the responses comparable, selected only those that were present in all 40 cities. # Analysis: Building a Network of Categories from Ego-centered Data

To transform the ego-centered network data into a whole network, we created a matrix of relationships among all pairs of actors using the measure of average intensity of collaboration between groups of egos and alters that were clustered according to the same criterion, that is, applying the same categories of types of actors. In so doing, the link between a pair of actors (e.g., business actors and trade unions) captured the average intensity of collaboration between the particular business actors sampled (egos) with any trade union that fulfilled the parameters considered in the questionnaire (alters). Vice versa, we captured the average intensity of collaboration of the trade unions that were surveyed with any business actors with whom the collaboration fits the framing of the questionnaire. To do so, the ordinal categories for the intensity of collaboration between each respondent and each one of the actors listed in the questionnaire were treated as a quantitative variable (no collaboration = 0, rare collaboration = 1, occasional collaboration = 2, intense collaboration = 3). This resulted in a weighted and directed complete network. *Bolíbar et al.* 119 10 Field Methods 0(0)

Once the adjacency matrix is built, the wide array of whole-network analysis techniques can be applied to the matrix and to the network construct. In our example of application, we computed measures of centrality (weighted in- and out-degree) and analyzed the subgroup structuring of the network. For the latter, conforming to a consolidated practice to identify cohesive subgroup structures in valued graphs (Wasserman and Faust 1994), we excluded the links that have a csv value lower than the median. Following Weidele and Brughmans' (2017) indications of how to perform cluster analysis using network data, we applied the Louvain method for community detection to weighted matrix data.<sup>3</sup> The Louvain method for community detection allowed us to identify clusters of nodes of actors with locally dense relationships that were weakly linked to each other (i.e., groups of actors with closely knit connections within the groups and sparse links between the groups).

# Results

The analysis of the network of categories brings out two interesting features of the whole governance network and the position of immigrant organizations within it regarding (1) the centrality of the actors and (2) the formation of subgroup structures.

First, immigrant organizations have a rather peripheral position within local organizational networks (Figure 1). The most central actors in terms of in-degree—i.e., those nominated more often and more intensively as collaborators by all respondents, are political actors, followed by welfare organizations and administrative actors (particularly those working on urban planning and integration). This was expected because political actors make policy decisions that affect everyone, thus many urban actors want to have the ability to inform such policy agendas. Similarly, actors from the local administration and welfare organizations provide services and resources and are thus attractive cooperation partners for the everyday work of many local organizations.

Second, the division of the network highlights an interesting pattern of community formation, which splits the network into two parts (Figure 2). The first part is formed by trade unions, public and private economic actors (business organizations and branches of the administration related to economic development and work), political actors and urban planning administrative actors. The second part is composed of welfare organizations, other diversity organizations, immigrant organizations and the branches of the administration that are related to migrants' integration.

This pattern reflects a segmentation between a cluster of organizational actors related to social and diversity issues and a cluster of economic and political actors, with immigrant actors being firmly positioned in the former

# Discussion and Conclusion

We have presented a technique for analyzing and visualizing collections of sampled ego network data as a whole network construct based on the relationships between categories or node attributes. The method is applicable to data collected through an adaptation of resource generators, position generators or aggregate relational data type of questions. It combines information on the connectedness of groups of particular ego actors (clustered together after data collection) with abstract groups or categories of alters that are predefined in the questionnaire (therefore, prior to data collection). In our empirical application, links between these categories are based on a calculation of the aggregated "average intensity of collaboration" between all egos of a particular category with a given type of alter. Links with a value lower than the median are not considered for some analyses to facilitate the visualization and analysis of the network of collaborations. Different measures could be applied to identify the edges, such as the percentage of egos establishing a given type of relationship or the mean number of actors known, as long as they neutralize or minimize dependency on the number of interviewees in each category. Different criteria could also be applied to simplify the network so that rare or infrequent collaborations do not mask the main established and most intense collaborations. Moreover, the network of categories can be analyzed in several ways, depending on the research question guiding its application.

By aggregating the ego and alter organizations in categories such as type of organizational actors, the network of categories removes the context sensitivity of every actor in their particular location to present only the connectedness among abstract and broad categories of actors. As a result, it misses the detail of particular cases' network positions, while retrieving the structuring of relationships along the categories. Given the nature of the information obtained through this method, the classifying schemes of categories should be theory based and refer to the most relevant social cleavages of the research field of application. Moreover, they should be defined prior to the start of fieldwork, and be consistent, exclusive, univocal, and applicable to both ego and alter actors.

The main advantage of our network of categories is that it overcomes the limitations of both whole networks and ego-centered network analytical approaches: It allows the overall network structure to be observed and insights to be gained into the contextual network effects, while also generalizing the results to a wider population.

We illustrated the applicability of our method by analyzing a collection of sampled ego network data on collaborations among relevant urban organizational actors in 40 French and German cities. The study aimed to determine the embeddedness of organizations that represent immigrant interests in local governance networks across different policy fields. By creating a network of categories, a whole network was represented and analyzed, providing information about the patterns of relationships of collaboration among all relevant types of organizational actors and the positions of immigrant organizations within that network. The results show that, while immigrant organizations have entered local governance networks in French and German cities, their position and influence remain peripheral and are mostly restricted to the immigrant integration policy field. A division appears in governance networks between the "hard" and "soft" policy fields that hinders immigrant organizations' interactions with economic and urban planning organizational actors.

The application of the network of categories widened the analytical scope of previous research on immigrant actors' networks, allowing research to go beyond the observation of first-degree dyadic relationship involving *Bolíbar et al.* 123 14 Field Methods 0(0)

immigrant organizations. Applying standard egocentric network analysis such as cross-sectional statistical multilevel models (Crossley et al. 2015 or Lubbers et al. 2010), in previous research we described and modelled the factors contributing to the existence and nature of particular ties from immigrant actors to other actors and ties of collaboration from nonimmigrant to immigrant actors (Schiller et al. 2020). While this approach yielded fruitful results, by building the network of categories, the results provide a wider comprehension of the overarching structures of their relationships that constrain immigrant organizations' networks. In other words, our method enabled the contextual structure of governance networks that surrounds networks from and to immigrant organizations to be seen, thereby connecting the empirical stance with the basic foundational aims of the structural approach of social network analysis (Wellman 1988).

This method could be applied to other research fields. For instance, it could be used to examine the commercial connections among a set of countries (e.g., the EU countries). A survey questionnaire to a representative sample of companies in each country should be implemented, where companies would be asked if they do business and how frequently with companies (any) from a list of countries (a list that should include all EU countries). Applying the method of the network of categories, then a network of business exchange among all EU countries could be constructed, visualized, and analyzed.

The method could also be applied to individuals. For instance, it would be useful to study the social connectedness or segregation among social profiles defined according to their occupational status. A sort of position generator should then be used to ask respondents about their acquaintance with people of different occupational status (defining them broadly). Aggregating that information as we propose with the network of categories, a network of acquaintance among occupational statuses could be created.

A limitation of our methodological construct is that the size or presence of the categories in the field will affect their chances to be nominated as collaborators (therefore bigger or more widespread actors will have a higher indegree in the resulting networks). In this sense, the commercial connections among EU countries would probably show a predominant role of German companies within European business and a peripheral role of Latvian companies and that would be explained, in part, because the pool of German companies with whom to collaborate is bigger than the Latvian one, regardless of the average likelihood of German and Latvian individual companies to attract business. Similarly, in our empirical application, if we had considered all actors of the administration as one single broad "administrative actor" (instead of differentiating three specific categories—i.e., "branches of the administration related to migrants' integration, economic development, and urban planning) its in-degree centrality would have been much higher. Researchers should take this into account and pay close attention to the definition of the "types" or "categories" so that relationships between them are comparable, and/or that the interpretation of the results takes that construct effect into account. Potential weights could also be applied if considered appropriate, depending on the research question and setting in which the method is applied.

The main disadvantage of the whole network construct is that the type of actor nodes do not refer identically to the same individual organizations in the sample and in the questionnaire list (e.g., while all local immigrant organizations are susceptible of being mentioned as actors with whom the respondents collaborate, not all of them responded to the questionnaire or were therefore part of the sample). Therefore, the reversed links between clustered actors are not perfectly comparable, as the nominated and nominating organizations are not exactly the same specific organizational actors. Nevertheless, if the sample selected is representative of the whole population of actors, the mismatch should be minimal. Future developments of this tool should delve into the procedures to ensure the representativeness of the sample for all types or categories of surveyed actors.

Despite the limitations, the potential of the method is large. As Smith (2012) argues, using a sample of individuals to make inferences about whole network structures enhances the feasibility to move beyond small, institutionally bounded populations to undertake comparative network work. In our example, we could reconstruct our networks of categories either by comparing cities and countries, or by identifying different typologies of networks based on the patterns of connections among the proposed categories.

The network of categories has the additional advantage of being based on a simple data-collection tool, which enhances its applicability and spread in different research contexts. It is more efficient, and its quality is less compromised by non-response than attempts to reconstruct whole networks by collecting information of the whole population of a given field. Compared to the other existing methods to aggregate sampled egonetworks into whole networks described in the article, our method requires data that can be obtained easily through short egonetwork data collection instruments such as adapted resource generators, position generators, or aggregate relational data, which are far faster, cheaper, and easier to use than name generators and name interpreters. Privacy in data collection and results visualization is also an advantage of the network of categories, particularly in the field of interorganizational networks.

In sum, devoid of complex calculations our method can be incorporated as a short module in a standard survey questionnaire and requires little time to retrieve the necessary information, while nonetheless providing results of high analytical value.
